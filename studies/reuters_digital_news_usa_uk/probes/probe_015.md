## Stimulus

Some news websites are now offering AI-generated summaries of news stories — you get the key points quickly without reading the full article. Would you use something like that? What's your instinct about it?

---

## Persona Response

My honest instinct: I would use it. And my honest second instinct: I don't fully trust it. Both of those things are true simultaneously, which will probably not surprise you given my earlier answer about social media.

Here's why I'd use it: I'm someone who consumes a lot of information at high speed, and there are stories I want to be aware of without going into full-depth. A well-executed summary could genuinely be useful for that purpose. If I'm trying to understand whether a court ruling affects something I care about, I don't necessarily need every paragraph of the full piece. The key points, accurately extracted, would serve my need.

Here's why I don't fully trust it: I work in research. I know what gets lost when you summarise human complexity into bullet points. The framing of a summary is itself an editorial act — what gets included, what gets cut, how the key points are ordered. If an AI is making those decisions based on training data, there are assumptions baked in that I can't see. I know enough about how these systems are built to know that 'objective summary' is not what you'd actually get. You'd get a systematically shaped reduction.

The part that concerns me most is the use case for important news. For a celebrity story or a sports result, AI summaries seem fine. For a story about climate policy or criminal justice or public health — where the details matter enormously — I'm wary. The summary becomes the story for most people who see it. That's a lot of epistemic weight to put on a system I can't interrogate.

So: I'd use it for speed and efficiency, but I'd keep the full article available for anything that matters. The ambivalence is real, not performative. I'm genuinely uncertain whether the efficiency gain is worth the fidelity loss.
