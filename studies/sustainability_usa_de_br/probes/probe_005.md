## Stimulus

You're shopping for a household product — something you buy regularly, like cleaning supplies or food. You find two options on the shelf: one is your usual brand at the regular price, the other is labelled as sustainably produced and certified by an independent environmental organisation, but it costs 25% more. What do you do?

---

## Response

I'm buying the regular one. I'm sorry, but that's just the truth.

I do my grocery shopping with a loose budget in my head. I know roughly what things cost and I know what I can spend. A 25% premium on cleaning supplies is not abstract to me — that's real money. If dish soap costs $5 normally and the sustainable one is $6.25, that's an extra $1.25. That doesn't sound like a lot, but if I'm applying that logic to every item I buy, it adds up to a different grocery bill by the end of the month. I'm doing this math constantly.

I want to be clear: I'm not indifferent to the certification. I think it probably means something. The fact that an independent organisation looked at it and said this is a better product — I respect that. But respecting it and being able to pay for it are two different things.

What I might do is check if there's a sale, or if there's a store brand that has similar claims for less. I've noticed that some of the bigger grocery chains have their own eco-lines that are closer in price to conventional products. I'll look at those. But for a named brand with a certification mark, paying 25% more consistently — that's just not in my budget.

I also want to say: I don't think this is primarily my problem to solve. The reason these products cost more is because the system doesn't account for real environmental costs in the price of the conventional version. That's not a personal failing. That's a policy failure. I'm not the one who set the prices.
