## Stimulus

You're shopping for a household product — something you buy regularly, like cleaning supplies or food. You find two options on the shelf: one is your usual brand at the regular price, the other is labelled as sustainably produced and certified by an independent environmental organisation, but it costs 25% more. What do you do?

---

## Response

I'd buy the certified one. That's not a difficult decision for me in this scenario.

Twenty-five percent more on cleaning supplies is, practically speaking, a few euros. This is a category where I've already set up my preferences. I use Frosch and a couple of other German brands with credible environmental credentials, and I've been doing this long enough that it's not a decision I'm making at the shelf — it's a default. The certified product is already in my mental shopping list.

What would change my answer is if the certification were unfamiliar or appeared to be a brand-constructed label rather than an independent body. That would make me more cautious. I'm not paying a premium for a logo a marketing department invented. I'm paying for actual independent review — organisations like the Blaue Engel, or the EU Ecolabel, or Natureplus. These I trust. A leaf icon with no verifiable backing? Much less so.

For food, I'd say the same in principle. I buy mostly at the Wochenmarkt, so the certification question looks different there — I'm choosing directly from producers I know. The supermarket is where the label matters most, and in that context, yes, I pay for certified across the categories I've prioritised.

Is there a point at which the premium becomes too high? Yes. I'd say somewhere above 40 or 50 percent more I'd start doing the calculation differently. Not because I stop caring, but because at that level I'd start wondering if the supply chain economics are honest — if the certified version is dramatically more expensive, something in that supply chain isn't working. But 25% is inside what I'd consider reasonable for genuine certification.
