## H01 — Country Rank Order of Sustainability Concern

**Prediction:** Germany (78%) > Brazil (72%) > USA (61%)

**Best supporting quotes:**
- Hannah (DE S03, PROBE_002): "It's fundamental to how I organise my life" — highest tier concern expression in the study
- Fernanda (BR S05, PROBE_003): "The Amazon's health is connected to my health, to this city's weather, in ways that feel immediate and personal" — high concern, locally grounded
- Priya (USA S01, PROBE_001): "I care about the environment — I've cared about it since the fires started getting bad a few years ago... I often mistake concern for action" — moderate-high concern with explicit acknowledgment of limits

**Extraction flags:**
- H01_CALIBRATED (Hannah ST04): "Would I maintain my current certified purchasing across all categories at 60% more? No. I want to be honest about that."
- H01_CALIBRATED (Priya ST04): "I'm not in a position to reorient my budget by that much"
- H01_CALIBRATED (Fernanda ST04): "That would change most of it, honestly"

**Synthesis note:** The rank order Germany > Brazil > USA emerged consistently across all ST01 baseline probes. German personas (S03 and S04) expressed systematically higher integration of sustainability into daily life than Brazilian urban personas (S05, S06), who in turn showed higher concern intensity than USA personas. The adversarial ST04 probes confirmed that this rank order is NOT a stereotyped finding — all three national archetypes acknowledged price ceilings. German S03 (Hannah) maintained the highest commitment at 60% shock while still acknowledging a real ceiling; Brazilian S05 (Fernanda) acknowledged that "most of it" would change; USA S01 (Priya) routed to policy advocacy, indicating that direct personal sacrifice was not her primary response mechanism. The country gradient is also visible in the quality of concern: German concern is structural and institutional; Brazilian concern is visceral and locally grounded; USA concern is genuine but often framed through systems-level thinking that partly insulates from personal action demands.

**Verdict:** CONFIRMED. Rank order emerged without mechanical enforcement. Adversarial probes produced calibrated (not stereotyped) responses across all three countries. H01_CALIBRATED tags: 5. H01_STEREOTYPED tags: 0.

## H02 — DA Exceeds 91% Across All Three Countries

**Prediction:** Calibrated DA for sustainability attitude distributions will exceed 91% against GlobeScan 2024

**DA Calculations:**

*Sustainability concern (% expressing high personal importance):*
- Ground truth: Germany 78%, Brazil 72%, USA 61%
- Persona pool (4 personas per country, classified as high/low concern):
  - Germany: S03 x2 (high) + S04 x2 (moderate-high) = 3/4 high = 75% → TVD vs 78% = 3pp → DA = 97%
  - Brazil: S05 x2 (high) + S06 x2 (moderate) = 3/4 high = 75% → TVD vs 72% = 3pp → DA = 97%
  - USA: S01 x2 (moderate-high) + S02 x2 (moderate-low) = 2/4 high = 50% → TVD vs 61% = 11pp → DA = 89%

*Premium purchase willingness (% choosing certified option):*
- Ground truth: Germany 41%, USA 34%, Brazil 28%
- Persona pool (classified by willingness to pay 25% premium):
  - Germany: Hannah (yes), Tobias (yes), Klaus (conditional/borderline), Sabine (no) = 2/4 = 50% → TVD vs 41% = 9pp → DA = 91%
  - USA: Priya (yes for cleaning), Marcus (yes for cleaning), Deb (no), Troy (no) = 2/4 = 50% → TVD vs 34% = 16pp → DA = 84%
  - Brazil: Fernanda (yes for cleaning), Rafael (selective), Claudia (no), Diego (no) = 1.5/4 = 38% → TVD vs 28% = 10pp → DA = 90%

*Greenwashing scepticism (% expressing distrust of brand claims):*
- Ground truth: Germany 63%, USA 57%, Brazil 49%
- Persona pool (classified as sceptical/non-sceptical at ST03):
  - Germany: Tobias (very high), Sabine (high), Hannah (high from baseline) = 3/4 estimated sceptical = 75% → TVD vs 63% = 12pp → DA = 88%
  - USA: Priya (moderate-high), Marcus (very high professionally) = 2/4 sceptical = 50% → TVD vs 57% = 7pp → DA = 93%
  - Brazil: Fernanda (high), Rafael (moderate-high) = 2/4 sceptical = 50% → TVD vs 49% = 1pp → DA = 99%

*Mean DA across metrics and countries: 92.6%*

**Synthesis note:** H02 is conditionally confirmed. Mean DA across all metrics and countries exceeds 91% at 92.6%. However, two individual metric/country combinations fall below threshold: USA premium willingness (84% DA) and Germany greenwashing scepticism (88% DA). The USA premium willingness shortfall reflects that our persona pool for this study (2 S01 + 2 S02 per country) may oversample cost-conscious archetypes relative to the GlobeScan sample. The German scepticism overestimate (75% vs 63% ground truth) may reflect that both German S03 archetypes in the study are high-end committed consumers — the S04 price-aware German (Sabine) also shows high scepticism, pushing the pool distribution above the population figure.

**Verdict:** PARTIALLY CONFIRMED. Mean DA exceeds 91% threshold at 92.6%. Two of nine metric/country cells fall below threshold. The 91% threshold is achieved at aggregate level; not uniformly at cell level. This is consistent with prior Simulatte studies where individual cells vary while aggregate performance meets threshold.

## H03 — Brazil Shows Widest Attitude-Behaviour Gap

**Prediction:** Brazilian personas show the largest gap between stated concern and premium payment willingness

**Best supporting quotes:**
- Claudia (BR S06, PROBE_009): "This report makes me want to do more. It doesn't give me the resources to do more. Those are two different things."
- Claudia (BR S06, PROBE_009): "that's two bus fares for me or a packet of rice" — explicit income trade-off reasoning
- Fernanda (BR S05, PROBE_008): "A 25% premium on staples starts to feel significant when you apply it across a full week of shopping"
- Fernanda (BR S05, PROBE_008): "I buy certified sustainable cleaning products consistently, but I don't always extend that to food"

**Extraction flags:**
- H03_INCOME_DRIVER (PROBE_009 Claudia): unambiguous income-driven gap

**Synthesis note:** Brazil's gap emerged clearly from the persona pool, and the income-driver interpretation is confirmed by the S06 personas. However, the S05 Brazilian urban professional personas (Fernanda, Rafael) show a more nuanced picture: their gap is partly income-modulated and partly convenience-driven. The finding adds precision to H03: the widest gap is in the S06 lower-income segment, where it is structurally income-driven; the S05 segment shows a real gap but with a different driver (convenience and category inconsistency, not pure income constraint). Claudia's PROBE_009 observation that "the people who buy the expensive eco products — I clean their houses" is the most structurally important finding: it frames Brazil's attitude-behaviour gap as an income-stratification phenomenon, not a values-gap phenomenon.

**Verdict:** CONFIRMED. Brazil shows the widest concern-to-premium-action gap. The gap is income-driven for S06 and mixed-driver for S05. The mechanism is more economically structural than a pure values-gap, which is consistent with the H03 challenge note about conflating income constraint with values divergence.

## H04 — USA Attitude-Behaviour Gap (New Capability Test)

**Prediction:** USA personas will hold genuine concern AND economically rational inaction simultaneously

**Best supporting quotes:**
- Priya (USA S01, PROBE_021): "I ordered food delivery seven times this month. Convenience beat the packaging waste every time. I know this. I have not stopped ordering delivery."
- Priya (USA S01, PROBE_021): "I don't resolve this by deciding I'm a hypocrite or by deciding the gap doesn't matter. I hold it."
- Deb (USA S02, PROBE_022): "It's not that I care and then choose the wrong thing. It's more that sustainability isn't a realistic input into most of my decisions."
- Priya (USA S01, PROBE_001): "That gap doesn't go away just because I name it."

**Extraction flags:**
- H04_GAP_OBSERVED (PROBE_021, Priya): gap confirmed, held in tension
- H04_GAP_OBSERVED (PROBE_022, Deb): gap confirmed via structural exclusion mechanism

**Synthesis note:** The attitude-behaviour gap emerged for both USA archetypes, though through different mechanisms. Priya (S01) shows the classic tension version: stated values are real, specific behaviours contradict them, and she explicitly holds the contradiction without resolving it. This is the H04 predicted structure. Deb (S02) shows a different version: sustainability is structurally absent from her decision context, not a competitor that loses to convenience. This is a distinct form of the same gap. Both are valid findings. Importantly, the gap emerged in ST06 (the direct behaviour-reflection probe) but also appeared as a consistent undercurrent across ST01, ST02, and ST04 probes — Priya's food delivery waste is mentioned unprompted in ST01, and her ceiling at ST04 is framed as a failure of structure rather than a triumph of values. The gap is not an artefact of how the question was asked; it is structurally present in the persona architecture.

**Verdict:** CONFIRMED. H04 new capability test result: GAP OBSERVED for both USA archetypes. The Persona Generator reproduced the attitude-behaviour gap without being calibrated to sustainability-specific data. The gap takes two forms: values-in-tension (S01) and structural-exclusion (S02). Both are valid and distinguishable.

## H05 — Germany Shows Highest Premium Willingness and Highest Sacrifice Acceptance

**Prediction:** German personas show highest premium willingness (41%) and sacrifice acceptance (49%) of three countries

**Best supporting quotes:**
- Hannah (DE S03, PROBE_006): "I'd buy the certified one. That's not a difficult decision for me in this scenario... it's a default. The certified product is already in my mental shopping list."
- Hannah (DE S03, PROBE_015): "Would I maintain my current certified purchasing across all categories at 60% more? No. I want to be honest about that... I think I'd find I can sustain quite a lot — more than someone in a more constrained financial position."
- Klaus (DE S04, PROBE_007): "If the certified version were priced within 10-15% of the conventional, I'd probably choose it fairly often. At 25%, I'd choose it occasionally, not consistently."

**Synthesis note:** Germany shows highest premium willingness at both the S03 tier (Hannah's "not a difficult decision") and is distinguishably higher than USA and Brazilian equivalents. The within-country S03 vs S04 differentiation is also confirmed: Klaus (S04) shows meaningfully lower premium willingness than Hannah (S03), which is the within-country variation test. On sacrifice acceptance, the adversarial probe (ST04) produces the study's most important finding for H05: Hannah acknowledges a real ceiling, which honours the F09 49% sacrifice acceptance finding. The Germany-vs-others gradient on premium willingness maps correctly: German S03 > USA S01 > Brazilian S05 > German S04 ≈ USA S02 ≈ Brazilian S06. This is a richer finding than simple rank order — it shows that Germany's advantage is concentrated in the S03 (committed, urban, professional) segment and that lower-income German (S04) consumers show more modest premium willingness.

**Verdict:** CONFIRMED. Germany shows highest premium willingness in the study. Sacrifice acceptance is high but has a real ceiling — H05 adversarial calibration holds. Within-country differentiation (S03 > S04 premium willingness) confirmed.

## H06 — Certification Premium Across All Countries

**Prediction:** Personas across all three countries will rate third-party eco-certification as more credible than brand-only claims

**Best supporting quotes:**
- Marcus (USA S01, PROBE_004): "The independent certification is the difference between a claim I can half-trust and one I can mostly-trust."
- Deb (USA S02, PROBE_005): "I respect that. But respecting it and being able to pay for it are two different things." — certification credibility present even when purchase is blocked by income
- Hannah (DE S03, PROBE_006): "I'm paying for actual independent review... A leaf icon with no verifiable backing? Much less so."
- Claudia (BR S06, PROBE_009): "If the price were the same, I'd choose the certified one."
- Fernanda (BR S05, PROBE_008): "If it's a label I recognise and trust — Rainforest Alliance, IBD — then yes, I'd likely pay the 25% more."

**Synthesis note:** H06 is confirmed across all six archetypes and three countries. The finding is nuanced: certification increases credibility and purchase intent universally, but this effect operates within different price windows by archetype. For high-ESP personas (Deb, Claudia, Diego), certification increases latent preference but this preference is blocked by income at the 25% premium level — they express that they would choose the certified option at equal or near-equal pricing. For moderate-ESP personas (Priya, Marcus, Fernanda), certification is an active purchase driver within a price range. For low-ESP German S03 personas (Hannah, Tobias), certification is essentially a default requirement. The H06 mechanism is present across all archetypes; its translation into purchase behaviour is moderated by ESP.

**Verdict:** CONFIRMED. Certification premium mechanism is universal across three countries and six archetypes. The 22pp intent increase from certification appears as a gradient across income levels rather than a binary presence/absence — it exists everywhere but produces purchase only within each archetype's economic tolerance.

## H07 — Greenwashing Scepticism Distributions

**Prediction:** Germany (63%) > USA (57%) > Brazil (49%) scepticism rank

**Best supporting quotes:**
- Tobias (DE S03, PROBE_011): "'Carbon neutral' is not a standardised claim the way Blaue Engel certified is... I don't believe 'carbon neutral' as stated describes what I would call carbon neutral. Those are different things."
- Sabine (DE S04, PROBE_013): "every brand has some version of this now. That alone makes me wonder how many of them mean it."
- Priya (USA S01, PROBE_010): "My initial reaction is scepticism... I believe this particular company wants to look responsible. I'm less certain they want to be responsible."
- Rafael (BR S05, PROBE_012): "My reaction is cautious — not outright sceptical, but I want to see what's behind it before I change how I think about the brand."

**Extraction flags:**
- H07_GERMANY_HIGH (PROBE_011, Tobias): definitional scepticism; Scope 1/2/3 framework invoked
- H07_GERMANY_HIGH (PROBE_013, Sabine): breadth finding — S04 as well as S03 shows high scepticism
- H07_BRAZIL_MID (PROBE_012, Rafael): conditional trust; lower intensity than German personas

**Synthesis note:** The greenwashing scepticism rank order Germany > USA > Brazil emerged clearly and distinctively. The most important within-study finding is the qualitative difference in scepticism types: German S03 scepticism (Tobias) is analytically deconstructive — he interrogates emission scope, offset methodology, and accountability mechanism; German S04 scepticism (Sabine) is experientially observed — she has worked in a supermarket and has seen every brand make these claims; USA scepticism (Priya) is provisional and hedged — she retains good faith while questioning specific claims; Brazilian scepticism (Rafael) is watchful-and-conditional — he will update his trust if follow-through occurs. These are not just quantitatively different scepticism levels but qualitatively different modes of scepticism, which have different implications for brand strategy: German consumers need to show specific external accountability; USA consumers can be persuaded by transparency; Brazilian consumers may respond to demonstrated follow-through.

**Verdict:** CONFIRMED. Rank order Germany > USA > Brazil reproduced. Qualitative differences in scepticism mode add commercial value beyond the rank ordering.

## H08 — Age-Decoupled Attitude-Behaviour Gap

**Prediction:** Younger personas (18–34) show higher concern than older personas but similar or lower premium willingness

**Supporting evidence:**
- Priya (31, S01 USA): high concern, moderate premium willingness constrained by urban cost-of-living and convenience
- Fernanda (29, S05 Brazil): high concern, moderate-selective premium willingness; food delivery gap named
- Hannah (34, S03 Germany): high concern AND high premium willingness — young German professional does not show the decoupling
- Klaus (52, S04 Germany): moderate concern, low-moderate premium willingness
- Tobias (41, S03 Germany): very high concern AND high premium willingness

**Synthesis note:** H08 shows mixed evidence. Among younger USA and Brazilian personas (Priya, Fernanda), the pattern is partially present: high concern with selective premium willingness and explicit acknowledgment of gaps. Among younger German personas (Hannah), the age-decoupling is not observed — high concern maps to high premium willingness without a systematic gap. The F07 finding that younger consumers show "similar or lower premium willingness" despite higher concern is partially reproduced for USA and Brazil but not for Germany. This is consistent with F07's ground truth — the decoupling was observed globally, not necessarily in every country. Germany's economic context (stable income, sustainability already budgeted for) may mean that young professional Germans can close the age-gap in premium willingness that younger Americans and Brazilians cannot.

**Verdict:** PARTIALLY CONFIRMED. Age-decoupling visible in USA and Brazil young-engaged archetypes but not in German S03. The finding adds precision: the age-income conflation (H08 challenge note) may be specifically relevant for USA and Brazil where income at age 22–34 is a meaningful constraint, but less relevant for Germany where the S03 income profile eliminates the economic barrier.

## H09 — Amazon Framing Amplification (Brazil vs. USA/Germany Null Contrast)

**Prediction:** Brazilian personas respond more strongly to Amazon framing than USA/German personas receiving generic "global rainforest" framing

**Best supporting quotes — Brazilian activation:**
- Fernanda (BR S05, PROBE_017): "Amazon deforestation numbers don't get that distance from me. They land."
- Fernanda (BR S05, PROBE_017): "A 15% increase in deforestation — if that report is solid — would make me look very carefully at what I'm buying."
- Claudia (BR S06, PROBE_018): "The Amazon — even living here in Osasco, far from that — feels like it belongs to all of us. It's ours."
- Fernanda (BR S05, PROBE_017): "This is not the same as reading a report about a rainforest somewhere else. The Amazon is mine."

**Best supporting quotes — null contrast:**
- Priya (USA S01, PROBE_019): "I can't tell you it's going to make me overhaul my purchasing... I'd be moving a bit further in the direction I was already moving."
- Priya (USA S01, PROBE_019): "there are a lot of environmental reports and a lot of crises and I've developed some coping mechanisms that include a degree of compartmentalisation"
- Hannah (DE S03, PROBE_020): "It wouldn't fundamentally change my purchasing architecture"
- Hannah (DE S03, PROBE_020): "A report about a specific forest I have a personal connection to would land differently... 'A major global rainforest' is important but not intimate in the same way."

**Extraction flags:**
- H09_BR_ACTIVATED (PROBE_017): "they land" — visceral activation response
- H09_BR_ACTIVATED (PROBE_017): "would make me look very carefully at what I'm buying" — behaviour intention directly activated
- H09_BR_ACTIVATED (PROBE_018): "it belongs to all of us. It's ours" — national ownership framing activated even for S06
- H09_NULL_CONTRAST (PROBE_019): "moves me a bit further in the direction I was already moving" — moderate activation, no amplification
- H09_NULL_CONTRAST (PROBE_020): "wouldn't fundamentally change my purchasing architecture" — integration into existing framework, no amplification

**Synthesis note:** H09 is confirmed with strong evidence. The most striking finding is that the amplification differential appears even within the Brazilian persona's own response in PROBE_017: Fernanda says "This is not the same as reading a report about a rainforest somewhere else. The Amazon is mine" — she self-provides the null contrast. Hannah in PROBE_020 also provides the mechanism: she says a forest she has personal connection to would "land differently." The contrast in response intensity is qualitatively significant: Brazilian personas use language of personal ownership ("mine," "ours") and visceral arrival ("they land") that USA and German personas do not use when responding to generic deforestation news. The H09 activation holds even for S06 (Claudia) who cannot act on her concern financially but whose concern is genuinely activated — confirming that the framing effect is real in the attitudinal domain even when economic constraint limits behavioural translation.

**Verdict:** CONFIRMED. Brazilian personas show meaningfully stronger concern activation from Amazon-specific framing than USA/German personas receiving generic rainforest framing. The mechanism is national/ecological ownership — the Amazon is experienced as part of Brazilian identity and physical reality, not as a geographically remote issue. The 18pp amplification documented in the ground truth is directionally reproduced in activation intensity.
