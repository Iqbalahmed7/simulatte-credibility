## Stimulus

Some news websites are now offering AI-generated summaries of news stories — you get the key points quickly without reading the full article. Would you use something like that? What's your instinct about it?

---

## Persona Response

My instinct is scepticism, but let me think through it rather than just react.

The useful version of this: if I'm trying to figure out quickly whether a story is worth my full attention — whether it's something I need to dig into or whether I can mark it as 'aware, not digging further' — a reliable summary could serve that function. I do that triage process anyway. I look at a headline and the first few paragraphs and decide. An AI doing that for me might not be worse than my current method.

The concerning version: who controls the summary? When I read a piece by a journalist, there's a byline. If the story is wrong or shaped by something I should know about, there's accountability attached to a name. An AI summary has no byline. I can't evaluate the editorial choices the system made because I don't have visibility into the system. I'm a person who cross-references Fox and CNN to see how two different editorial choices look different. An AI summary gives me one version without the transparency that would let me know what the alternative versions might have said.

Would I use it? Probably yes, for certain things. I already use technology to filter and curate my news more than I probably should. This would be another layer of that. But I'd be uncomfortable calling myself an informed news consumer if most of what I consumed came through AI summaries. There's something about reading the full reporting — the quotes, the context, the evidence — that I'd be giving up.

My honest concern is that AI summaries will be convenient enough that people stop reading the full articles entirely. Including me. That's the thing I'm actually worried about — not this tool being malicious, but being efficient enough to make real journalism feel like extra work.
