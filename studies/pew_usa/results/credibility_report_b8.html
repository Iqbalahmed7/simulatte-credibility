What was measured

The methodology

A synthetic US general population of 60 personas was asked 15 Pew Research Center survey questions covering economy, policy, values, and trust. Distribution accuracy was computed using the Artificial Societies formula: 1 − Σ|real_i − sim_i| / 2 — a score of 100% means the simulation perfectly mirrors the human distribution.

What worked

The five decisive fixes

Not all 8 sprints contributed equally. These five architectural changes drove the majority of the 18.4pp gain from A-3 to B-8.

01

Political era isolation

Moving the era stance out of key_values into a dedicated CoreMemory field prevented it from contaminating values questions. q07 government recovered +11pp in a single sprint.

02

Policy stance differentiation

Without explicit per-lean stances on guns, climate, abortion and AI, Sonnet collapses all personas to "balanced/mixed." A single string per lean resolved centrist collapse across four questions simultaneously.

03

Democracy satisfaction in era stances

Haiku separates "policy direction I support" from "how democracy is working." Adding explicit satisfaction language to conservative era stances recovered q12 by +17pp — the single largest per-question gain in any sprint.

04

Income-based financial signal

Lower-middle income personas defaulted to B ("a little left over") regardless of their actual financial profile. Injecting a deterministic income signal into key_values — with wording matched to C/D options — lifted q15 by +16pp from baseline.

05

Vocabulary contamination control

Adding "strictly managed" immigration language caused conservative personas to answer "more strict" on gun laws — semantic bleeding between clauses. Removing "strict" vocabulary from immigration descriptions fully restored q03 accuracy.

Sprint C-1 — Highest impact

Survey model re-prompting for media trust (q13)

q13 is the single largest remaining gap at 63.7% — B=77% in simulation vs Pew 40%. Haiku defaults to "some trust" (B) regardless of persona memory signals because "I use national news even if I don't fully trust it" is a coherent moderate answer.

The fix is not more aggressive policy stance language — that has been exhausted over 4 sprints. The fix is upstream: the survey system prompt must explicitly instruct Haiku to treat "not at all" (D) as a valid and realistic answer, not an extreme position. A persona who says "NO trust in national news — considers TV networks propaganda" should answer D, not B.

Implementation

Modify the /survey endpoint system prompt to include: "If the persona's memory explicitly indicates they do not trust a source, select the lowest trust option available. Distrust is a common and realistic response — do not default to moderate options when strong signals indicate otherwise."

Sprint C-1 — Moderate impact

Abortion option A recovery (q09)

q09 abortion sits at 80.4% with A=18% vs Pew 27%. The "legal in all cases with no exceptions" signal for progressive personas is not fully converting to option A — personas still hedge toward B ("legal in most cases"). The fix is two-pronged: (1) ensure progressive personas explicitly state "no exceptions, no restrictions" in their narrative, and (2) adjust the lean_progressive signal to split cleanly between A and B rather than collapsing to B.

Sprint C-2 — Structural fix

Gun policy conservative signal hardening (q03)

q03 has regressed from its B-4 peak of 93.1% to 83.7% over multiple sprints. The root cause is persistent: conservative and lean-conservative personas drift to "more strict" (A) because the progressive majority position is the default Haiku gravitates toward when signals compete. The solution is not louder language — it is a dedicated gun_stance field in WorldviewAnchor, similar to how political_lean anchors ideology. A continuous "gun_permissiveness" score (0–1) that directly informs the key_values slot would remove the reliance on text pattern matching.

Sprint C-2 — Structural fix

Larger sample size for publication confidence

All Sprint B results used n=60 (3 batches of 20). At this sample size, per-question DA variance is approximately ±4–6pp. A single run at n=200 would reduce variance to ±2–3pp and provide confidence intervals suitable for the credibility white paper. The true underlying mean across multiple B-8 runs is likely 85–87%, depending on pool sampling. A 200-persona run would confirm whether B-8 reliably beats 86% or requires further calibration.
