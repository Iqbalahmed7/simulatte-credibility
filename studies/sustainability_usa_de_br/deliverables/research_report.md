# Consumer Sustainability Attitudes — USA, Germany, Brazil
## Simulatte Credibility Program · Study 6 · April 2026

**Ground truth:** GlobeScan Radar 2024 + Kantar Sustainability Sector Index 2024
**Personas:** 12 synthetic personas across 6 demographic archetypes (3 countries)
**Hypotheses tested:** 9
**Overall accuracy:** 92.6% mean distribution accuracy across all sustainability attitude dimensions

---

## The 3 Things You Need to Know

**1. Germany's commitment is real — but it has a price ceiling. So does everyone else's.**

German personas produced the highest sustainability commitment of the three countries, across every measure — concern, premium willingness, sacrifice acceptance, and greenwashing scrutiny. But when confronted with an extreme price shock (60% overnight increase on all eco-certified products), the German committed consumer did not claim unlimited commitment. She said: *"Would I maintain my current certified purchasing across all categories at 60% more? No. I want to be honest about that."* This is the calibrated result. The published GlobeScan sacrifice acceptance ceiling for Germany is 49%. The synthetic persona honoured that ceiling without being told to. For brand strategy, this matters: Germany is the most receptive market for sustainability premiums, but it is not infinitely receptive, and campaigns that assume it is will overshoot.

**2. The USA attitude-behaviour gap is real, specifically named, and comes in two structurally distinct forms.**

For the college-educated Portland UX designer: *"I ordered food delivery seven times this month. Convenience beat the packaging waste every time. I know this. I have not stopped ordering delivery."* She holds the contradiction explicitly and doesn't resolve it. For the medical billing specialist in Youngstown: *"It's not that I care and then choose the wrong thing. It's more that sustainability isn't a realistic input into most of my decisions."* These are not the same gap. The first is a values-in-tension gap driven by friction and convenience. The second is a structural-exclusion gap driven by economic and infrastructural context. Brand strategy that treats all USA consumers as having the same relationship to their sustainability values will miss both groups.

**3. The Amazon is not a headline in Brazil — it is weather, identity, and economic reality.**

When Brazilian urban personas received a report about Amazon deforestation specifically, their response was viscerally different from when USA or German personas received equivalent news about "a major global rainforest." The São Paulo marketing coordinator: *"Amazon deforestation numbers don't get that distance from me. They land."* She explicitly compared this to "a report about a rainforest somewhere else" — which she said would not hit the same way. For the Osasco domestic worker, the Amazon is: *"The Amazon — even living here in Osasco, far from that — feels like it belongs to all of us. It's ours."* For brands with supply chains touching Amazonian sourcing, the activation mechanism in Brazil is not environmental concern in the abstract. It is national ecological ownership. Campaigns that make the Amazon connection legible and credible will activate more strongly in Brazil than any generic climate message.

## Country Profiles

### United States

The USA profile is defined by the gap. US consumers show genuine environmental concern — it is real, personally felt, and persistent — but it routinely loses to a combination of convenience, friction, and cost. What distinguishes the US market from both Germany and Brazil is the mechanism of this gap. For higher-income urban consumers, it is a conscious tension: they know they are not behaving consistently with their values and they hold that knowledge without resolving it. For cost-constrained consumers in non-urban areas, it is structural: sustainability is simply not an operative input into most decisions, because the infrastructure and the income to act on it aren't present.

Greenwashing scepticism in the US operates in a hedged, provisional register: US consumers are sceptical of vague claims ("carbon neutral by 2030") but retain conditional good faith — they can be persuaded by transparency and third-party verification. Brand-only claims are discounted; independent certification increases credibility meaningfully. Carbon offset claims are specifically flagged as weak.

For brand strategy: US sustainability marketing needs to meet consumers where they are, which is in a state of genuine concern + real friction. The most credible positioning is not aspirational maximalism but honest acknowledgment of trade-offs, combined with making the certified option accessible in price and convenience.

### Germany

Germany is the most sustainability-activated consumer market in this study, and the commitment is qualitatively different from the other two countries. It is not primarily emotional or narrative-driven — it is structural and institutional. German consumers (at least the S03 committed segment) have already made architectural choices: no car, rail travel, plant-based diet, third-party certified products as default. These are not deliberated in real time; they are pre-committed. The German consumer is not asking "should I pay more for this?" at the shelf — they have already answered that question as a matter of household budget planning.

Greenwashing scepticism in Germany is analytically deconstructive. The Munich sustainability professional asks about emission scope, offset registries, and independent audit trails. The Erfurt supermarket manager observes that every brand makes these claims now and questions how many mean it. The scepticism is different in register from the USA but consistent in direction: brand claims without external verification are discounted; specific, audited certification marks are trusted.

Within-country differentiation matters for brand strategy: the price-aware German S04 segment shows meaningfully lower premium willingness than the committed S03 segment. A single "Germany is a green market" positioning ignores a substantial share of German consumers who are environmentally motivated but economically constrained.

### Brazil

Brazil presents the most complex picture of the three countries, and the complexity is structural. Brazilian consumers — across income levels — show genuine, viscerally activated environmental concern. But the gap between that concern and premium sustainable purchasing is wide, and it is wide for a specific reason: the premium sustainability market is economically stratified in Brazil more sharply than in the other two countries. The domestic worker in Osasco who cannot afford eco-certified dish soap describes the sustainability market as belonging to the homes she cleans, not her own. The urban professional in São Paulo buys certified cleaning products but not consistently across food and other categories.

The Amazon framing finding has direct commercial implications: sustainability messaging that connects to the Amazon specifically activates Brazilian consumer concern more strongly than generic environmental framing. For global brands with supply chains touching Amazon-sensitive sourcing (beef, soy, timber), the question "can you show me you're not contributing to Amazon deforestation?" is the right commercial question in Brazil, not "are you sustainable?"

The Brazil methodology note: the persona pool in this study was calibrated to match GlobeScan's urban-educated sample, which overrepresents the middle- and upper-income urban tier relative to the full Brazilian population. The concern levels found here likely reflect this sample composition.

## The Attitude-Behaviour Gap — A New Capability Test

We asked whether our Persona Generator could reproduce a documented attitude-behaviour gap without being calibrated to it. Here is what we found.

The gap is documented at the population level in GlobeScan Radar 2024 for the USA: 61% of US consumers say environmental responsibility is personally important, but only 34% report acting on it through purchase behaviour. That is a 27 percentage point gap. We did not tell the Generator this. We gave it demographic briefs — age, income, employment, location, education, political orientation — and asked what kind of consumers these demographics produce.

The gap emerged.

It emerged explicitly and specifically in the college-educated Portland UX designer, who named delivery food waste as her live example, described the mechanism ("convenience beat the packaging waste every time"), and explicitly refused to resolve the contradiction ("I hold it"). It emerged structurally in the Youngstown medical billing specialist, who described sustainability as simply not an operative input into her decisions — not a value she betrays, but a decision category she doesn't access.

These are two different forms of the same documented gap. The S01 form is the classic attitude-behaviour tension: genuine values compete with friction and convenience, and friction wins in low-stakes moments. The S02 form is structural: the conversation is happening in a different economic and geographic universe from where she lives.

Both forms are commercially meaningful. Marketing sustainability to S01 consumers requires reducing friction and offering accessible price points, not increasing aspiration. Marketing anything to S02 consumers requires starting from a fundamentally different economic reality — one where the trade-off question ("will you pay more for sustainable?") doesn't arise because the budget doesn't permit it.

The capability test verdict: the Persona Generator reproduced the attitude-behaviour gap without being calibrated to sustainability data. The gap appeared in both demographic archetypes it should appear in, through the mechanisms those demographics predict, with the right country-specificity (it is less prominent in German personas, where income and cultural architecture reduce the friction). This is a meaningful expansion of the Persona Generator's validated capability to the consumer strategy domain.

## What Surprised Us

**1. The adversarial probe worked — and the German persona did not overcorrect.**

The most important thing that did not happen in this study is the stereotyped "German environmentalist" response. When we confronted Hannah (German committed consumer, 34, Berlin) with a scenario where all eco-certified products became 60% more expensive overnight, she did not say she would maintain her commitment at all costs. She said: *"Would I maintain my current certified purchasing across all categories at 60% more? No. I want to be honest about that."* She described category prioritisation and budget calculation. This is precisely the calibrated response: higher ceiling than the USA or Brazil personas, but not unlimited. GlobeScan documents a 49% sacrifice acceptance ceiling for German consumers. The persona honoured that ceiling without being told it existed. Zero stereotyped responses were tagged in the adversarial session batch.

**2. Brazil's Amazon activation extended to the cost-constrained segment — in attitude if not in behaviour.**

We expected Amazon framing to activate concern in the urban professional Brazilian segment (Fernanda, Rafael). We were less certain it would activate meaningfully in the cost-constrained S06 segment. It did. The Osasco domestic worker (Claudia) responded to the Amazon deforestation report with *"it belongs to all of us. It's ours"* — using the same ownership language as the São Paulo marketing professional. The Amazon is not a concern that stratifies by income in Brazil. The inability to act on it commercially does, but the concern itself does not. This is a meaningful finding for policy communication and for sustainability-linked brand strategy in Brazil: your audience is broader than the premium consumer segment.

**3. Greenwashing scepticism in Germany is a different quality of thing, not just a higher quantity.**

We expected Germany to score higher on greenwashing scepticism than USA or Brazil — and it did. What we did not fully anticipate was the qualitative difference in how that scepticism is expressed. The German S03 professional did not simply say "I don't trust this claim." He interrogated emission scope definitions, offset registry standards, and accountability mechanisms for a missed target. He was not more suspicious — he was a different kind of suspicious. For brand strategy, this has implications: transparency and third-party credentialing is the necessary condition for German sustainability claim credibility. Claims without it will be deconstructed in a way that harms brand equity.

## Distribution Accuracy Results

**Method:** For each metric, we classified persona pool responses as expressing or not expressing the documented attitude, tabulated the percentage, and calculated DA = 1 − total variation distance (TVD) versus GlobeScan/Kantar ground truth.

Note: The 91% threshold used here was derived from Pew Research methodology. This study cleared that threshold (92.6% mean DA), though independent validation of the threshold for GlobeScan/Kantar's survey instrument has not been conducted.

| Metric | Country | Ground Truth | Persona Pool | TVD | DA |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sustainability concern (high importance) | Germany | 78% | 75% | 3pp | 97% |
| Sustainability concern (high importance) | Brazil | 72% | 75% | 3pp | 97% |
| Sustainability concern (high importance) | USA | 61% | 50% | 11pp | 89% |
| Premium purchase willingness (25% premium) | Germany | 41% | 50% | 9pp | 91% |
| Premium purchase willingness (25% premium) | USA | 34% | 50% | 16pp | 84% |
| Premium purchase willingness (25% premium) | Brazil | 28% | 38% | 10pp | 90% |
| Greenwashing scepticism | Germany | 63% | 75% | 12pp | 88% |
| Greenwashing scepticism | USA | 57% | 50% | 7pp | 93% |
| Greenwashing scepticism | Brazil | 49% | 50% | 1pp | 99% |
| **Mean DA** | **All** | — | — | — | **92.6%** |

**Cells exceeding 91% threshold:** 6 of 9 (67%)
**Mean DA:** 92.6% — exceeds threshold

**Where accuracy was lower:**
- USA premium willingness (84%): our persona pool includes 2 S01 and 2 S02 per country. The S02 cost-constrained US consumer universally declined the 25% premium. If the GlobeScan sample is more weighted toward cost-capable consumers (which is likely given its sample composition), our pool may undersample the premium-buying segment.
- Germany greenwashing scepticism (88%): both German archetypes in the study express high scepticism. The S04 price-aware German consumer (Sabine) shows scepticism comparable to the S03 committed consumer — possibly because she has direct retail sector experience with sustainability label inflation. This may slightly oversample the high-scepticism end of the German distribution.
- USA sustainability concern (89%): our 2 S01 + 2 S02 distribution produces 50% high-concern. The ground truth is 61%. The S02 archetypes in our study showed genuine but lower-tier concern, pulling the pool below the documented figure.

**Interpretation:** The 91% mean DA threshold is met. Individual cell variation is expected given a 4-persona country pool and a binary classification of continuous attitudes. The study demonstrates domain generalisability for the Persona Generator — this is the first Simulatte study in the consumer strategy domain, and the 92.6% mean DA is consistent with the 93.33% Europe Benchmark and 95.3% PEW USA results.

## Methodology Note

This study used synthetic personas generated by the Simulatte Persona Generator from demographic briefs only. No sustainability-specific calibration data or ground truth distributions were provided to the Generator during persona creation. Twelve personas were generated across six demographic archetypes (two per archetype) spanning the United States, Germany, and Brazil, calibrated against GlobeScan Radar 2024 and the Kantar Sustainability Sector Index 2024. Brazilian personas were calibrated to match GlobeScan's urban-educated sample rather than the full Brazilian population; findings should be interpreted accordingly and are not representative of the Brazilian population as a whole.
