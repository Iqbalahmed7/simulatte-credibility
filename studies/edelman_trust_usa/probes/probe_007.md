## Transcript

**Stimulus delivered:** Two people are speaking publicly about the safety of a new type of medical treatment. The first is a scientist at a university research centre. The second is the CEO of the pharmaceutical company that makes it. Both say the treatment is safe. How much weight do you give each person's statement? Who do you find more credible, and why?

**Margaret:**

The scientist substantially outweighs the CEO for me, and the gap is large enough that I want to explain it carefully rather than just state it.

The scientist at a university research centre has structural independence from the financial outcome of this treatment. Their professional incentive is to publish accurate findings and not have them contradicted later. Academic reputation is built slowly and destroyed quickly — a major methodological error, or worse, a result that doesn't replicate, can define a career. That accountability structure is what makes scientific credibility work. It's not that scientists are morally superior people. It's that the incentive architecture rewards honesty and punishes error, at least in the long run.

The CEO has the opposite structure. The treatment being safe and effective is directly tied to the company's market capitalisation, regulatory approval, and liability exposure. They may genuinely believe it is safe — CEOs often do believe their own products are good. But the financial alignment is so complete that the statement "it is safe" coming from a CEO is almost structurally unverifiable. It's like asking someone whether their own decision was the right one. They'll usually say yes.

I should be honest about the limits of this. Scientists can have financial conflicts too — grant money, industry funding, consulting relationships. If this university researcher has pharmaceutical industry funding and the treatment is the thing their lab worked on, I weight their credibility lower than an independent academic reviewer. I also weight peer review above single-person claims — what I actually trust is the body of evidence, not any individual spokesperson, scientist or otherwise.

So: scientist, substantially more credible. CEO, not without relevance — they have information about manufacturing process and real-world adverse event tracking that a basic researcher might not — but credibility on the safety claim itself, where the financial interest is most directly implicated? Very low.
