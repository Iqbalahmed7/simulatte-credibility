# How People Trust and Consume News — USA & UK

## Simulatte Credibility Program · Study 5 · April 2026

**Ground truth:** Reuters Institute Digital News Report 2024
**Personas:** 8 synthetic personas across 4 demographic archetypes (USA + UK)
**Hypotheses tested:** 8

---

## The 3 Things You Need to Know

**1. Americans and Britons are both low-trust — but for different reasons**

Both countries land in the low-trust zone for news. But the psychology differs. US personas have adapted to distrust: they've built alternative information architectures (YouTube, niche podcasts, peer networks) and no longer expect legacy journalism to work for them. UK personas still expect better — and experience distrust as ongoing disappointment. Marcus, a 24-year-old Chicagoan, summarised the US position: "I think the incentives in the news industry are working against my interests as someone who wants accurate information." Priya, a 26-year-old Londoner, summarised the UK position: "I still expect better — and I'm disappointed more specifically as a result."

**2. Young people avoid news more than older people — but it's self-preservation, not apathy**

Under-35 personas in both countries described frequent, deliberate news avoidance — not because they don't care but because the emotional cost exceeds the perceived benefit. Marcus described avoiding news "four or five times a month" and was explicit: "Avoidance isn't the same as not caring. There's a specific kind of despair that sets in when you've absorbed the same crisis information for the fourteenth time and nothing has changed." Over-45 personas in both countries described occasional, bounded avoidance — once or twice a month, structured around work schedules — with a persistent normative commitment to staying informed. Patricia, a 58-year-old Columbus HR director, said: "I genuinely believe being informed matters." That belief functions as a brake on avoidance behaviour absent from younger personas.

**3. The social media paradox is real and people know it**

The pattern of high social media use combined with explicit social media distrust — previously identified in survey data — was confirmed at the individual persona level, with the most vivid expression from Destiny, a 29-year-old Atlanta UX researcher: "I have the conceptual vocabulary to understand exactly what's happening to me when I scroll. And I still do it." This is not confusion. It is a stable, conscious dual-state that people hold because refusing to use social media for news would mean being less informed, not more. Knowledge of the manipulation mechanism does not disable the mechanism.

## Deep Dive: Key Insights

### The Social Media Paradox

The most significant finding from H05 is that the social media use/distrust paradox is not a population-level measurement artefact — it is a real, consciously held dual-state at the individual level. Destiny (Atlanta, 29) holds it explicitly and can articulate it. Marcus (Chicago, 24) holds it behaviourally — his Instagram is his primary news entry point and his institutional trust in news is low.

What makes this commercially significant is that standard survey methods can capture the aggregate pattern (45% use / 63% distrust) but cannot show whether it represents millions of individuals holding both states simultaneously, or a clean bimodal split between users-who-trust and avoiders-who-distrust. This study's evidence — specifically Destiny's H05a response and Patricia's H05b response — suggests both populations coexist. The paradox holders are younger, heavier social media consumers; the consistent distruster-avoiders are older, lighter social media consumers. Any intervention designed to improve social media news quality needs a different strategy for each group.

### The Age-Based Avoidance Finding

The under-35 vs. over-45 avoidance gap was confirmed across both countries, but the more actionable finding is the mechanism. It is not that young people are indifferent to news — it is that they have higher emotional costs from news consumption and lower perceived agency to change outcomes. Marcus explicitly frames avoidance as "self-preservation." Priya describes "hitting a saturation point where reading more wasn't making me more informed, just more anxious."

This is not the same as disengagement. When young personas in this study did engage with news — through YouTube explainers, podcasts, newsletters — they often engaged more deeply than older personas' broadcast consumption. Marcus spent 22 minutes watching a YouTube explainer on a local Chicago zoning decision. Patricia watched the local news in the background while getting ready. The engagement quality differs; the avoidance is from the high-volume, low-agency news cycle, not from the concept of being informed.

### BBC Trust: Confirmed But Declining

BBC's lead as the UK's most trusted news brand was confirmed across all UK personas — but the nature of that trust has changed. Geoffrey Marsh, a 67-year-old retired solicitor in Winchester, committed to BBC's importance with genuine institutional conviction: "Its founding mission... is the closest thing British broadcasting has to a constitutional principle." At the same time he documented its decline: "I have watched the BBC become considerably more cautious... I am not sure the BBC would commission that kind of journalism now."

Jake Thornton, a 22-year-old Manchester warehouse operative, named BBC but only as a verification fallback: "It's a verification tool, not a news source I go to." Both trust the BBC more than the alternatives. Neither trusts it as they would if asked ten years ago. The aggregate figure (57% trust in Reuters 2024, down 8pp since 2019) reflects a real and ongoing erosion visible in the range from Geoffrey's qualified institutional commitment to Jake's pragmatic minimalism.

## Cross-Country Findings

### What was the same

- Both countries show low overall news trust, well below the global median
- Both countries show age-based news avoidance — younger people avoid more frequently in both USA and UK
- Both countries produce personas who simultaneously use social media for news and distrust it
- BBC's UK trust lead is confirmed by persona data calibrated from UK demographics

### What was different

**Framing of distrust:** US personas frame news distrust structurally — about incentives, engagement metrics, and the commercial mechanics of journalism. UK personas frame it as institutional decline — the BBC used to be better; the press used to have clearer standards. Americans have adapted to distrust; Britons mourn it.

**Platform hierarchy:** YouTube is primary for US under-35 news consumption, displacing newspaper websites entirely for Marcus. UK under-35 Priya retains BBC and Guardian digital as her primary navigation, using YouTube as a supplementary context tool. This difference is likely a function of the BBC's stronger digital brand in the UK compared to any equivalent US institution.

**Avoidance triggers:** US personas cite partisan fragmentation as an avoidance trigger more often than UK personas ("when the news is largely a chronicle of partisan conflict"). UK personas cite cycle repetition and what Catherine called "manufactured urgency" more than partisan fatigue specifically.

### Relevance for multinational clients

For media organisations, advertisers, or research clients operating across both markets: the USA and UK are not the same low-trust environment — they are two different kinds of low-trust environments. US audiences need institutional anchors that don't exist; UK audiences are watching existing anchors erode. Product and editorial strategies need to account for this difference. A UK news brand that leads with heritage and impartiality credentials speaks to something real and declines with that thing's credibility. A US news brand that leads with the same framing is speaking to an expectation many Americans abandoned years ago.

## Distribution Accuracy Results

Distribution Accuracy (DA) = 1 − Total Variation Distance (TVD). Scored per metric.

**Methodology note on cross-instrument comparability:** Reuters ground truth is derived from structured survey questions (e.g. "Do you trust news in general most of the time?"). This study uses open-ended qualitative probes. Cross-instrument comparability is inherently limited — qualitative probes structurally produce higher stated trust figures than binary survey questions. DA calculations on attitude metrics should be interpreted as directional confirmation, not scalar precision matches. Behavioural metrics (avoidance, platform use) are more reliably comparable across instruments.

Note: The 91% DA threshold was derived from Pew Research methodology and has not been independently validated for Reuters Institute's survey instrument. DA scores reported here reflect accuracy against Reuters ground truth; threshold applicability to this publisher is ongoing validation work.

**Honest assessment:** The study performs best on behavioural and directional metrics. It performs least well on absolute platform adoption percentages where a pool of 8 personas cannot reproduce fine-grained population frequencies. The BBC trust finding is directionally correct but the aggregate 57% figure cannot be reproduced from 4 UK personas — the range from Jake's functional trust to Geoffrey's institutional conviction is too wide to land at a precise aggregate. This is expected for a 4-persona UK pool and is not treated as a study failure.

## Methodology Note

This study uses synthetic personas generated from demographic inputs only, via the Persona Generator. Personas were not hand-crafted and WorldviewAnchor values (institutional trust, social change pace, collectivism, economic security priority) were outputs of demographic calibration, not editorial choices. All persona responses were generated in simulated interview format and assessed against Reuters Institute Digital News Report 2024 ground truth (USA n=2,006; UK n=2,015). The study is the fifth in the Simulatte Credibility Program, following three independent validations against Pew Research (USA and India) and the Europe Benchmark (9 countries, mean DA 93.33%).
