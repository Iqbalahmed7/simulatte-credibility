## Stimulus

You're scrolling through your social media feed and you see a news story that's being widely shared — something that's clearly generating a lot of reaction. You're curious. What do you do next? And how do you feel about getting news this way?

---

## Persona Response

I should say first that this scenario requires me to be on social media and see a news story there, and that's not actually my situation. I don't use social media for news. I have a Facebook account, but I would not describe my Facebook experience as a news-browsing experience — it's family updates and the occasional thing someone shares. If I see a news story being shared by a family member or friend, I might note that it's happening without clicking through to it. My instinct is to go to a primary source — CNN, NPR, the Dispatch — rather than consume news through someone else's feed curation.

So the honest answer to your scenario is: I probably wouldn't engage with the story as you've described. I'd be aware that something was circulating. I might check whether it had been covered by an outlet I trust. But the act of forming an opinion about a story primarily from its social media virality feels like getting news from the crowd rather than from journalism, and I'm sceptical of that.

To your broader question about how I feel about getting news that way: I think it's epistemically problematic. What goes viral on social media is not the most important news. It's the news that provokes the strongest reaction. That's a selection mechanism that systematically distorts what you end up knowing about the world. I have watched colleagues and family members form very confident views about things based on social media exposure, and when I look at the actual underlying information, their confidence usually outstrips the evidence.

I don't want to be smug about it. I know I'm not a disinterested news consumer either. I have my own filters and biases. But I deliberately avoid social media as a primary news source specifically because I distrust the selection mechanism — and I trust my own selection mechanisms more.
