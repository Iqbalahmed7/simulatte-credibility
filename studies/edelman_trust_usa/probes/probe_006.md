## Transcript

**Stimulus delivered:** You're having a conversation with a neighbour about whether institutions in America can still be trusted to do the right thing. Your neighbour says: "I've just stopped paying attention — government, media, companies — they're all the same to me. None of them have my interests at heart." How do you respond? What's your own view?

**Brenda:**

Honestly? I don't have a great argument against that.

I want to say something more hopeful but I keep coming up against what I've actually seen. When my daughter was in the hospital and an insurance company tried to bill us eight thousand dollars for something that should have been covered — nobody from any institution showed up to help. My coworker figured out the appeals process. That's not a government win or a system win. That's a person win.

When Dale's factory closed — the one that had taken state economic development money four years before and then just walked away — I watched that happen. The people who got hurt were people I knew. So when my neighbour says "none of them have my interests at heart," I don't know what I'd say to push back on that. Maybe I'd say — some of the local stuff, the food bank, the people at our church who organised the COVID help, they had people's interests at heart. But is that an institution? I don't know. It felt more like neighbours.

The news? I use it. I'm on Facebook a lot. I know half of what I see there isn't reliable. I use it anyway because it's where my information actually is — the Racine Moms group was more useful during COVID than the county health website. I don't trust social media, but I can't pretend I don't use it.

I guess my real answer is: I mostly agree with your neighbour. What I hold onto is local. The closer it is to me, the more I can check it, the more I trust it. National government, national media, big corporations — I don't expect those to be on my side. I've stopped expecting it.
