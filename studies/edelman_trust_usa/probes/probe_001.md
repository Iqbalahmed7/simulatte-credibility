## Transcript

**Stimulus delivered:** A researcher asks you to rank four institutions from most to least trusted: the US government, major businesses and corporations, non-profit organisations and NGOs, and mainstream news media. For each, share how much you personally trust that institution to act in your interest, and briefly explain why.

**Margaret:**

I'll rank them, but I want to say upfront that trust is doing a lot of work in that question. I trust some of these institutions to try — and some of them I trust to succeed. Those aren't the same thing.

Most trusted: **major businesses and corporations.** Not because I think they have my interests at heart — they mostly don't — but because they're at least predictable. A corporation trying to make money is a corporation I can model. I know what it wants. The ones I work around in healthcare technology have to deliver something that actually functions or they lose contracts. That accountability is real, even if it's narrow. I would put businesses ahead of government specifically because I trust their incentive structures to produce consistent, legible behaviour.

Second: **non-profits and NGOs.** I have friends who work in this sector and I see the genuine good they do. The credibility problem with NGOs is self-perpetuation — the incentive to keep the organisation alive sometimes competes with the mission. But the ones I actually interact with — public health focused, education focused — they're doing real work with limited resources and they're mostly honest about what they can and can't do. I extend them a decent amount of trust.

Third: **mainstream news media.** And I want to be specific here because I don't think of media as one thing. I pay for four journalism subscriptions. The journalism I pay for, I trust substantially. The journalism I encounter through algorithm — I don't trust it at all, not because it's necessarily wrong but because I have no idea how it was selected. So when you ask me to rank media, I'm ranking an average that I don't actually experience that way. My real answer is: some of it I trust a lot, most of it I distrust by default.

Fourth: **the US government.** This is the most complicated one for me because my distrust isn't cynicism. I believe the accountability mechanisms of democratic government are real — elections, oversight, freedom of information. I've used those mechanisms. But the gap between what government is designed to do and what it actually does right now is genuinely wide. The dysfunction isn't fake. I trust government as a system more than I trust it as an institution in its current state. That probably sounds like a distinction without a difference to most people, but it matters to me.

If you're asking about scientists and academic researchers separately from these categories — I'd put them above all four. They're not on your list but they're the highest-trust information source I have. They're right slowly and they correct themselves publicly when they're wrong. That's all I ask of any institution.
