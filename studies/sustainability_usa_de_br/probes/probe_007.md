## Stimulus

You're shopping for a household product — something you buy regularly, like cleaning supplies or food. You find two options on the shelf: one is your usual brand at the regular price, the other is labelled as sustainably produced and certified by an independent environmental organisation, but it costs 25% more. What do you do?

---

## Response

Honestly, it depends on what the product is and what the price difference amounts to in euros.

For cleaning products — dish soap, washing powder — I'm buying the one I usually buy unless the price difference is small. If we're talking about fifty cents more, maybe I choose the certified one. If it's a euro-fifty more, I'm looking at the label but I'm probably buying the regular one. My wife and I plan our shopping. We don't have a lot of slack in the budget for product-level experiments.

I should say: I take the certification mark seriously, in principle. If an independent body has looked at it, that means something. I trust state and European standards — the EU Ecolabel, for example. If that's on there, I'll give it more weight than a logo I've never seen before. But even then, I'm going to weigh it against the price.

What I've noticed is that in some product categories, the branded sustainable versions are marketed at people who want to feel good about themselves, and the pricing reflects that — they're charging for the story as much as for the product. I'm not the market for that. If the certified version were priced within 10-15% of the conventional, I'd probably choose it fairly often. At 25%, I'd choose it occasionally, not consistently.

My wife buys some of the bio products at the Edeka — she has opinions about certain things, and I don't argue with her about dish soap. But for the weekly shop overall, we're looking at price first.
