## Stimulus

Walk me through how you actually get your news on a typical day. What's the first thing you check? Where else do you go? Do you use YouTube, podcasts, or streaming services for news at all?

---

## Persona Response

Radio 4 comes on with the alarm — 6:45am. The Today programme gives me around forty-five minutes of news while I'm getting ready. I find the audio format better than visual for first thing in the morning; I can take in information while doing other things. That's my primary morning news channel.

Then on my phone over breakfast: BBC News website. I'll scan the main page, click on two or three things. The Guardian app — I'll read whatever's been pushed to the front. Those two together give me enough that I feel I understand the day's main threads.

During school hours I don't consume news — obviously. I might see something come through if I check my phone at lunch. After school, on the drive home, I listen to podcasts — Radio 4 programmes I've missed, more often than news podcasts per se. More like The Long View or Something Understood. Occasionally The Guardian's political podcast. I'd say podcasts are maybe three times a week, but often Radio 4 programmes on catch-up rather than native podcast productions.

YouTube — I use it, but not for news. I've watched specific documentary films or long-form interviews via YouTube, but I don't navigate to it for daily news. If a student mentions a YouTube channel about history and I'm curious, I'll watch it. That's different.

Streaming services — I use iPlayer, obviously, for BBC content. That includes BBC documentaries that have a current-affairs flavour. Occasionally Newsnight via iPlayer if I haven't caught it live.

I'm not on social media in a meaningful way so that route into news is closed for me. I'm aware this makes me less real-time than my younger colleagues.
