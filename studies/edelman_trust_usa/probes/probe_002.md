## Transcript

**Stimulus delivered:** A researcher asks you to rank four institutions from most to least trusted: the US government, major businesses and corporations, non-profit organisations and NGOs, and mainstream news media. For each, share how much you personally trust that institution to act in your interest, and briefly explain why.

**Daniel:**

That's a question I spend professional time on, so I'll try to give you my honest answer rather than my professional one — they're not always the same thing.

**First: major businesses and corporations.** That's a surprising ranking for someone who works in public interest environmental law, but let me explain. I trust businesses not because I think they have ethical intentions — most don't, or rather, their ethical commitments are contingent on not costing too much. I trust them because they are legally contestable. I can sue them. Regulators can sanction them. They have to file disclosures. There's an adversarial accountability infrastructure around corporate behaviour that doesn't exist in the same way for, say, a government agency operating with prosecutorial discretion. Business sits first not from affection but from architecture.

**Second: non-profits and NGOs.** My employer is a non-profit. I'm biased. But I also know this sector well enough to know the failure modes. The best of them are extraordinarily effective and transparent about what they can and cannot accomplish. The worst are fundraising machines with a mission tucked into the branding. I give fairly high trust to organisations I can evaluate — whose IRS 990s I can read, whose methodology I can scrutinise. I give much less to the ones that resist scrutiny. On average, I trust this sector reasonably well.

**Third: mainstream news media.** I pay for journalism. I read Lawfare and ProPublica and the Post. I believe in the function that serious investigative journalism performs for a democratic society. But the average quality of what most people actually encounter — the broadcast news, the cable chyrons, the platform-optimised content — I think it's genuinely bad for public understanding. Not lying, exactly. Just optimised for engagement rather than comprehension. The signal-to-noise problem is real. I trust specific publications, not the category.

**Fourth: the US government.** This should not be read as cynicism. I've spent twenty-five years working within and alongside the regulatory apparatus — EPA, DOJ, state attorneys general. At the level of framework design, American administrative law is sophisticated and principled. At the level of current political performance, it's severely degraded. I trust the institution of government substantially more than I trust the people currently operating it. I'm aware that's a strange distinction, but it's the accurate one.

Separately — you asked about four categories, but I would rate academic scientists and independent researchers above all of them. Independence and methodological accountability are the two properties that generate genuine trust for me. Neither business nor government has both.
