## Stimulus

You're shopping for a household product — something you buy regularly, like cleaning supplies or food. You find two options on the shelf: one is your usual brand at the regular price, the other is labelled as sustainably produced and certified by an independent environmental organisation, but it costs 25% more. What do you do?

---

## Response

It would depend on what I know about the certification. If it's a label I recognise and trust — Rainforest Alliance, IBD, something I can look up and find is a real independent body — then yes, I'd likely pay the 25% more for cleaning products. I've done exactly this already with one brand I found last year.

For food, it's a bit different. São Paulo is expensive. My income is decent by Brazilian standards but the cost of living here means I'm more careful than the number on my payslip might suggest. A 25% premium on staples starts to feel significant when you apply it across a full week of shopping.

Where I've stopped and thought about this: I buy certified sustainable cleaning products consistently, but I don't always extend that to food, even though I think the environmental impact of food choices is actually larger. That inconsistency bothers me a bit. The cleaning product feels like a category where the claim is legible — the certification says something specific. In food, I've found the labelling more confusing and harder to trust.

I work in marketing and I know that some of the sustainability messaging in food is genuinely weak. "Natural" means nothing. "Eco-friendly" without certification means nothing. I look for the specific body name on the label. If I don't find it, I'm more likely to buy what I usually buy.

On the Brazil point: we have so much natural wealth and such complicated relationships with how it's managed. When I buy something with a Rainforest Alliance certification that includes Brazilian supply chains, that feels more personally meaningful than a generic claim about carbon offsets. The local connection matters to me.
