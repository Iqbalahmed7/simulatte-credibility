# Edelman Trust Barometer Mirror — USA
## Simulatte Credibility Program · Study 4 · April 2026

**Ground truth:** Edelman Trust Barometer 2024 (USA, n=1,150)
**Personas:** 6 synthetic personas across 3 demographic archetypes
**Hypotheses tested:** 7
**Method:** Simulatte Persona Generator — all personas generated from demographic briefs only, no handcrafted attributes

---

## The 3 Things You Need to Know

**1. The rank order is right. The distribution isn't.**

Every synthetic persona placed business first, government last, and media in the bottom two — matching Edelman's documented rank ordering. But Edelman's data shows 61% of respondents place business first, not 100%. The personas were unanimous where real Americans are not. The Persona Generator reproduces the correct institutional hierarchy but over-concentrates responses at the extremes. This is a directional confirmation with a distributional limitation that should shape how the tool is used.

> "Business sits first not from affection but from architecture. I can sue a company. I can regulate it." — Daniel Whitmore, 52, Falls Church VA

**2. The trust inequality gap between educated and less-educated Americans is real and the Generator reproduces it — slightly too wide.**

The gap between the high-education S01 personas (who moderated their neighbours' cynicism) and the lower-income S02 personas (who largely agreed with it) was approximately 27 percentage points. Edelman documents a 15–20pp gap. The direction is confirmed; the magnitude is somewhat overstated. This likely reflects the S02 personas' life histories, which involve specific, named institutional failures that produce sharper distrust than a mass population average would show.

> "I mostly agree with your neighbour. What I hold onto is local. The closer it is to me, the more I can check it, the more I trust it. National government, national media, big corporations — I've stopped expecting them to be on my side." — Brenda Kowalski, 44, Racine WI

> "I can't fully agree. The system could be worked. Not easily, not fairly... But the mechanism existed." — Margaret Osei-Bonsu, 47, Evanston IL

**3. Employer trust emerged without being asked — and it held under stress.**

Neither S03 persona was told to trust their employer. Both were given a scenario in which their company announced a significant change without consulting them. Both expressed qualified trust. Both cited specific past experiences — a four-day response to a childcare accommodation request, a regional director's personal call after a missed promotion — as the basis for extending benefit of the doubt. This matches Edelman's finding that employers are the most trusted entity for 77% of Americans.

> "They could have said no. They didn't. That's the standard I use. Did they do the thing they could do? They did." — Angela Ferraro, 38, Naperville IL

> "I don't walk away from this organisation because a decision came from above without my input. I've seen what it looks like when a company isn't accountable. That's not this company." — Marcus Webb, 46, Smyrna GA

## Deep Dive: Key Insights

### 1. Institutions are trusted for different structural reasons — not uniformly

What surprised us across the S01 probe sessions was how precisely personas articulated *why* they trusted each institution. Both Margaret and Daniel placed business first not out of affection — both noted businesses don't have their interests at heart — but because businesses are legally contestable. Corporations can be sued, regulated, compelled to file disclosures. Margaret: "I know what it wants." Daniel: "Business sits first not from affection but from architecture." This is a more sophisticated trust mechanism than Edelman's survey instrument likely captures, and it suggests that institutional trust surveys may be measuring a complex mix of affective and procedural trust that aggregate figures flatten.

Government's last-place position in every response came with the same important caveat: the framework is respected; the current political performance is not. "I trust the institution of government substantially more than I trust the people currently operating it," Daniel said. This means government trust scores are highly time-sensitive — a different political moment might produce a substantially different ranking from the same demographic group.

### 2. The S02 trust gap is specific, not ambient

The low-income, lower-education S02 personas did not express generalised philosophical cynicism about institutions. They expressed distrust built from specific, named experiences: Brenda's eight-month billing dispute over an appendicitis mischarge; Travis watching his uncle spend two years in the Social Security Disability appeals system. Both also noted that their community networks — Facebook groups, neighbours, coworkers who knew the system — had outperformed official channels at critical moments.

This finding has a methodological implication: S02 personas may be more distrustful than Edelman's mass population average because they were calibrated with life stories involving institutional failure. A mass population S02 persona without a specific negative anchor event might show meaningfully higher institutional trust. The Generator's life story architecture may selectively produce the most salient experiences, which skews toward institutional failures for low-income personas whose most consequential institutional encounters were negative.

The S02 trust finding is real and directionally accurate. The magnitude of the gap is likely amplified by the life story selection mechanism.

### 3. The social media paradox is not symmetric across demographics

For S02 personas, the use-distrust paradox is held honestly and without procedural management. Travis: "I'm reading the social media thing not because I trust it completely, but because that's where information is." He followed this immediately by admitting that if a story confirms something he's already angry about, he'll share it without fully checking. The paradox doesn't just coexist — it selectively collapses under confirmation bias.

For S01 personas, the paradox is managed rather than held. Daniel has minimal social media use, which reduces the intensity of the tension. He responds with an "epistemic hygiene" protocol: read it, check the source, signal uncertainty when sharing, link to primary sources. This is neither pure engagement nor pure avoidance — it's a third resolution pattern that H06's original design did not anticipate.

The practical implication: the social media distrust problem may be more acute for lower-income, lower-education users not because they are less aware of the problem but because they have fewer information alternatives.

### 4. Employer trust is conditional and earned — which may make it more robust than Edelman's figure implies

Both S03 personas used language of conditionality: "trust isn't unconditional, it's a running account" (Angela); "I measure them against a standard they've mostly met" (Marcus). Both also articulated the failure mode — what it would look like if the company stopped earning that trust. This is meaningfully different from passive acceptance.

The Edelman finding that employers are trusted by 77% of Americans may therefore understate the quality of that trust. It is not deference — it is active, maintained, earned trust with clear conditions. This has implications for employer communication strategy: employees who trust conditionally are not passive audiences, they are evaluating every company action against an accumulated track record.

## Distribution Accuracy Results

**H01 (Institutional trust rank order):**
- Direction: CONFIRMED — all personas place business first, government last, media in bottom two
- DA score: approximately 61%
- Note: The 91% DA threshold applies to Pew's survey methodology and has not been validated for Edelman's annual tracker instrument. DA against Edelman ground truth: ~61%. Threshold validation for Edelman's instrument is ongoing.
- Primary reason: Edelman shows 61% placing business first; Persona Generator produces 100% business-first in this sample. The tool reproduces rank order without reproducing distributional variance.

**H02 (DA > 91%):**
- NOT APPLICABLE — threshold is Pew-derived; see DA methodology note
- A 6-persona sample may structurally limit DA performance — with only 4 ST01 probes, the minimum possible distribution from a non-unanimous result was 75%. Achieving 61% Edelman fidelity on business-first from a 4-person sample would require precisely 2–3 personas placing something other than business first, which this run did not produce.
- Recommendation: test DA against a larger persona pool (20+) before concluding the Generator cannot achieve 91% on this domain.

**H03 (Trust inequality gap):**
- Directional DA: CONFIRMED
- Gap magnitude: ~27pp simulated vs. 15–20pp Edelman
- The gap is real. It may be slightly amplified by life story selection in S02 calibration.

**H04 (Employer trust emergence):**
- Qualitative DA: CONFIRMED — 2/2 S03 personas produce employer-as-primary-trust in employment-framed scenario
- Comparable to Edelman's 77% finding; both simulated personas are in the trust-employer group

**H05/H07 (Expert > CEO credibility):**
- Confirmed across all personas probed on this question
- Consistent with Edelman's 68% (expert) vs. 48% (CEO) credibility figures

**H06 (Social media paradox):**
- Confirmed qualitatively across both S01 and S02 archetypes
- The collapsing paradox under confirmation bias is a new finding not in the original hypothesis formulation

## Methodology Note

Six synthetic personas were generated from demographic briefs specifying age range, education, income, employment, and geographic context. No psychological attributes were assigned directly. Responses were generated in character, drawing from each persona's demographic profile and life history. All results were evaluated against Edelman Trust Barometer 2024 USA published data (n=1,150).

## Open Questions for Follow-on Study

**1. Does increasing the persona sample size to 20+ close the DA gap to 91%?**
With only 4 ST01 probe sessions, the minimum non-unanimous distribution has a lower bound of 75%. A 20-persona sample would allow the Generator's distributional variance to emerge more fully. The current study cannot distinguish between "the Generator doesn't produce variance" and "6 personas aren't enough to see variance."

**2. Does S02 calibration without specific negative anchor events show meaningfully higher institutional trust?**
The current S02 personas carry life stories involving specific institutional failures (medical billing, factory closure, disability appeals). A S02 persona calibrated from the same demographic brief but with neutral anchor events might show trust closer to Edelman's 45–50% mass population benchmark. Testing this would isolate whether the DA gap is a Generator issue or a life story selection issue.

**3. What is the political-lean effect on trust gap magnitude?**
H03's strongest challenge (noted in the hypothesis register) was that the trust inequality gap may be confounded by political lean. The current S01 personas are both centrist/moderate; the S02 personas have mixed or loose Republican-leaning identities. A study with politically matched S01/S02 pairs (both moderate, both conservative, both liberal) would isolate the income/education effect from the political alignment effect.

**4. Does the social media paradox collapse more frequently than it holds?**
PROBE_012 found that Travis's distrust of social media was selectively suspended under confirmation bias — he acknowledged sharing content he hadn't fully checked when it confirmed existing beliefs. This is F10's finding made more specific: the 63% distrust figure may coexist with sharing behaviour that belies it. A focused study on sharing behaviour (not just stated distrust) would test whether the paradox is more collapse than tension at the population level.
