**Stimulus delivered:** Your company has just announced a significant policy change that will affect how you work day-to-day. You haven't been consulted about it, and you have some concerns about how it will play out. A colleague asks you: "Do you trust that the company is making the right call here?" What's your honest answer?

**Angela:**

Honestly? My honest answer is: probably yes, with some real concerns about execution.

Here's what I mean. I've been here six years. Six years is enough time to know how this company makes decisions. And my read — based on what I've actually seen — is that when they make changes, they're usually thinking about it. They're not always right, and they don't always explain themselves as clearly as I'd like. But they've followed through on things that mattered to me. When I had the childcare situation with the return-to-office thing, they said yes within four days. That's not a company that ignores what its employees need.

So when I hear about this new policy and I have concerns — which I do — I try to separate two questions: Do I trust their process? And do I think the specific outcome will be good for me? Those aren't always the same answer. I trust the process more than I can predict the outcome.

What bothers me is the no-consultation piece. That part doesn't feel right. I'm a team lead — I'm the person who translates policy into how a team of twelve actually works. Not asking me what the downstream effects look like seems like a gap in their process. I'm going to raise it. I think I can raise it because I've built enough credibility here to be heard.

But trust the company overall? Yes. With reservations. And I'll be watching how this rolls out, because trust isn't unconditional. It's a running account.

The alternative — assume bad faith, disengage, start looking for something else — that's not where I am. I've seen what it looks like when a company doesn't do right by its people. This company isn't that.
