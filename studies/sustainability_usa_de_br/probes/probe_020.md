## Stimulus

[USA/GERMANY VERSION]: A report has just come out showing that deforestation in a major global rainforest has increased by 15% this year. Environmental groups are calling it a crisis. As a consumer, does this change how you think about your purchasing choices — what you buy, which brands you support?

---

## Response

It's important and it concerns me, but I want to be careful about how I answer, because my honest response is that this is part of a stream of concerning environmental news rather than a single event that changes my behaviour step by step.

Deforestation is something I'm already factoring into some of my choices. The supply chains that drive it — primarily soy and beef, and to a lesser extent timber and palm oil — I've been aware of and trying to navigate for several years. A new report showing a 15% increase confirms that the problem is getting worse. It doesn't create new information about which consumer choices matter. I already knew that reducing meat consumption reduces deforestation pressure. I already knew that palm oil sourcing is complicated.

Does it motivate me? Somewhat. It would make me look more carefully at a palm oil certification I was uncertain about. It might make me more persistent in talking to someone about supply chain sourcing at a product I buy regularly. But it wouldn't fundamentally change my purchasing architecture — I've already made the choices I can make within my life as it's structured.

I should say: a report about a specific forest I have a personal connection to would land differently. The Schwarzwald, if it were under this kind of threat, would hit me harder. "A major global rainforest" is important but not intimate in the same way. I don't think that's irrational — proximity and personal attachment are real components of how humans respond to environmental news.
