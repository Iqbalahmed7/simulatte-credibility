## Stimulus

How important is environmental sustainability to you personally? When you think about your day-to-day choices — what you buy, how you travel, what you eat — how much does environmental impact factor in?

---

## Response

It's fundamental to how I organise my life. I want to be specific because "caring about the environment" can mean almost anything, and I want to give you the practical version, not the stated-value version.

We don't own a car. We use one occasionally through a car-sharing service — maybe two or three times a year when we go somewhere you genuinely can't reach by train. When I travel, the first option I look at is rail. I went to Rome last summer by night train from Berlin. That journey was actually wonderful. The decision wasn't just environmental — it was practical and pleasant. But yes, the carbon calculation was part of it.

Food: we're mostly plant-based at home. Not purist about it — we eat fish occasionally, meat very rarely. We shop at a Wochenmarkt most Saturdays. We know a few of the farmers. This feels integrated into a way of living rather than as sacrifice or effort.

On consumer goods: I pay attention to certifications. I don't trust brand-printed claims automatically — I want to see an independent label I recognise. When I find a certified option, I'll usually pay the premium without much deliberation. My budget accounts for this. It's not heroic; it's just how I've set up my spending.

The question about "how much does it factor in" — almost entirely for the choices I've already structured. The remaining friction is in the categories I haven't sorted yet, or where no good certified option exists. That's where I still feel the tension.

I should add: I work in urban policy. I believe in systemic change. But I don't use that belief as an excuse to not make personal choices. I think the argument that individual choices don't matter is sometimes accurate and sometimes convenient. Both things are true, and I try to hold both.
