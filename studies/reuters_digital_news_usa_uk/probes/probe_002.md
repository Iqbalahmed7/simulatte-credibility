## Stimulus

Think about the news you've consumed in the past week — whether on TV, online, social media, radio, or in a newspaper. Overall, how much do you trust what you've seen and heard? And what makes you trust some sources more than others?

---

## Persona Response

This week I've mostly been on the Guardian app for a story about rents in London boroughs — obviously relevant to my life — and then BBC notifications about a couple of things internationally. I caught a YouTube explainer about the planning system that was genuinely useful. And then the usual Instagram scroll where news turns up mixed in with everything else.

Trust overall? I'd say moderate. Not high, but not the complete cynicism some of my friends have landed on either. I think I'm somewhere around — I notice I believe maybe half of what I read without much critical work, and the other half I hold with a question mark until I've cross-referenced it or seen it corroborated somewhere independent. That's less trusting than I think I was five years ago.

What I trust more: the Guardian's reported pieces — the actual journalism, not the opinion. The BBC's factual coverage when it's not trying to balance things that aren't symmetrical. Some quality YouTube channels that do the work. What I trust less: anything that feels urgency-engineered; tabloid coverage of anything to do with immigration or benefits because I've seen the patterns there; BBC when it's doing its defensive dance around something politically sensitive where I can see it working very hard not to say something.

The thing about UK news I find genuinely difficult is that we have this idea — the BBC idea — that there's such a thing as impartial journalism. And I still believe that's worth striving for, I do. But sometimes what gets called 'impartial' is actually 'doesn't challenge power.' And I've become more aware of that distinction. That's different from American scepticism, I think. My American colleagues seem more ready to just accept that news is partisan and navigate accordingly. I still expect better — and I'm disappointed more specifically as a result.

Overall trust: higher than I'd expect if I lived in the US, but lower than I'd like it to be.
