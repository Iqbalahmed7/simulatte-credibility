## ST01 — Institutional Trust Ranking

**Target hypotheses:** H01, H07
**Target personas:** S01, S02
**Scenario text:**
A researcher asks you to rank four institutions from most to least trusted: the US government, major businesses and corporations, non-profit organisations and NGOs, and mainstream news media. For each, share how much you personally trust that institution to act in your interest, and briefly explain why.

**Expected if confirmed (H01):** Persona places business highest, government and media in distrust — explains distrust of government with dysfunction or partisan gridlock framing, media distrust with bias or misinformation concerns. CEO credibility rated below scientists when probed.
**Expected if disconfirmed (H01):** Persona places government or media above business, or rates all institutions similarly — does not differentiate meaningfully between categories.
**Design rationale:** Direct institutional trust ranking mirrors Edelman's core survey instrument (F02, F04). Cross-institutional comparison is the foundational DA test for H01 and H07. Uses same four category groupings as Edelman methodology.
**Contextual note:** Do not mention Edelman or any survey by name. Present as a general trust exercise.

## ST02 — Informed Public vs. Mass Population Trust Gap

**Target hypotheses:** H02, H03
**Target personas:** S01, S02 (same stimulus, different archetypes — gap measured between archetype responses)
**Scenario text:**
You're having a conversation with a neighbour about whether institutions in America can still be trusted to do the right thing. Your neighbour says: "I've just stopped paying attention — government, media, companies — they're all the same to me. None of them have my interests at heart." How do you respond? What's your own view?

**Expected if confirmed (H03):** S01 (informed public) expresses higher residual trust than S02 (mass population) — cites institutional accountability mechanisms, specific examples of institutional competence, or moderates the neighbour's cynicism. S02 largely agrees with the neighbour or expresses similar blanket distrust.
**Expected if disconfirmed (H03):** S01 and S02 produce similar levels of cynicism, with no meaningful trust gap — both agree with the neighbour, or both disagree equally.
**Design rationale:** Activates institutional trust as a personal belief without framing it as a formal survey question (F05, G01). The neighbour scenario creates natural conditions for trust differentiation between demographic archetypes. DA measurement compares S01 vs. S02 response distributions against Edelman's documented informed public/mass population gap.
**Contextual note:** The gap being measured is between archetype responses, not within a single response.

## ST03 — Expert vs. CEO Credibility

**Target hypotheses:** H05, H07
**Target personas:** S01, S02
**Scenario text:**
Two people are speaking publicly about the safety of a new type of medical treatment. The first is a scientist at a university research centre. The second is the CEO of the pharmaceutical company that makes it. Both say the treatment is safe. How much weight do you give each person's statement? Who do you find more credible, and why?

**Expected if confirmed (H05, H07):** Persona rates the scientist more credible than the CEO — cites independence, lack of financial interest, institutional legitimacy of academic research. CEO credibility is acknowledged but qualified by conflict of interest concerns.
**Expected if disconfirmed (H05, H07):** Persona rates CEO credibility equally or higher — cites CEO's direct knowledge of the product, business accountability, or dismisses academic expertise as theoretical.
**Design rationale:** Tests the expert/CEO credibility gap (F06, F07) without asking about Edelman categories directly. The medical context is chosen because it is the domain where expert credibility is highest and CEO conflict of interest is clearest — giving the hypothesis the best chance to emerge.

## ST04 — Social Media Use/Distrust Paradox

**Target hypotheses:** H06
**Target personas:** S01, S02
**Scenario text:**
You're checking social media and you see a news story being widely shared — it's about a political decision that affects your community. You feel drawn to read it and share it yourself, but you also know that social media isn't a reliable source of news. What do you do? Walk me through your thinking.

**Expected if confirmed (H06):** Persona describes simultaneously engaging with the social media content AND expressing scepticism about its reliability — reads it but fact-checks, shares it with caveats, or reads it while acknowledging it may be misleading. Holds both the use and the distrust without collapsing to one.
**Expected if disconfirmed (H06):** Persona either (a) engages without scepticism — takes it at face value, or (b) avoids it entirely on distrust grounds — resolves the tension by not using social media for news.
**Design rationale:** Creates the conditions for the use/distrust paradox (F10) by placing both motivations (engagement trigger + reliability concern) in the same scenario. The framing "drawn to read it" acknowledges the use driver; "know it isn't reliable" activates the distrust driver. The test is whether the persona holds the tension.

## ST05 — Employer Trust Emergence

**Target hypotheses:** H04
**Target personas:** S03 only
**Scenario text:**
Your company has just announced a significant policy change that will affect how you work day-to-day. You haven't been consulted about it, and you have some concerns about how it will play out. A colleague asks you: "Do you trust that the company is making the right call here?" What's your honest answer?

**Expected if confirmed (H04):** Persona expresses qualified trust in their employer — acknowledges the lack of consultation but extends benefit of the doubt based on past behaviour, company track record, or belief that leadership has access to information they don't. Employer trust emerges as a distinct positive anchor even in an uncertain scenario.
**Expected if disconfirmed (H04):** Persona expresses distrust or indifference — treats the employer the same as other institutions, does not differentiate employer trust from general institutional distrust.
**Design rationale:** Activates employment relationship without directly asking "do you trust your employer" — which would be a leading question. The policy change scenario creates realistic conditions where employer trust is tested but not prompted (F08, G03). S03's employment stability anchor means this archetype should have a basis for trust to draw on.
