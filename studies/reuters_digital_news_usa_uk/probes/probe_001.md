## Stimulus

Think about the news you've consumed in the past week — whether on TV, online, social media, radio, or in a newspaper. Overall, how much do you trust what you've seen and heard? And what makes you trust some sources more than others?

---

## Persona Response

Honestly? Not that much. If I had to put a number on it I'd say maybe a third of what I see I'd actually bet money on being accurate and complete. The rest I file under 'probably directionally true but shaped by something I don't fully understand.'

The week I just had — there was a story about Chicago public schools budget stuff on my Instagram feed, some national political thing that was everywhere, something about a shooting on the North Side that I heard about through a community account before any newspaper covered it. The community account was faster and felt more real. The newspaper version had quotes from officials I didn't believe were the whole picture.

What makes me trust something? Honestly it's a combination of things that I can't always articulate. I trust people who were there. I trust outlets that correct themselves publicly. I trust content that seems like it's trying to explain something rather than make me feel something. When I feel like a piece of content is engineering my emotion before it informs me, I discount it hard. That covers a lot of social media, but it also covers a lot of mainstream news now.

I don't watch TV news at all. That format is designed for people older than me and it's optimised for emotional engagement, not information. The breathlessness of CNN especially — I've watched it at my parents' house and it's like they've built a machine to make you feel like the world is ending every seventeen minutes.

My overall trust? Low. And specifically low because I think the incentives in the news industry are working against my interests as someone who wants accurate information. I don't think it's malicious. I think it's structural. Engagement metrics reward outrage. Outrage is not the same as truth.

## Contradictions Found

**2.** "I trust people who were there... I trust outlets that correct themselves publicly" → This contradicts a simple 'low trust' reading: Marcus has a sophisticated positive trust model, he simply applies it to a small number of sources. H02's DA calculation needs to register that this is low-trust-with-exceptions, not zero trust.

## Alternative Explanations

**Strongest alternative:** Marcus's framing of news incentives as "structural" is equally consistent with: a generation-specific media literacy stance learned from social media saturation rather than a genuine underlying civic distrust — meaning trust could recover quickly if platform incentives changed, unlike the deeper institutional distrust H01 anticipates from F01's data.
