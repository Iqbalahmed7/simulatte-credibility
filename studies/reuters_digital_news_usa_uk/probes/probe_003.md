## Stimulus

Some people find themselves actively avoiding the news at times — turning off notifications, skipping news websites, or just not wanting to read or watch it. Have you experienced this? How often does it happen, and what triggers it for you?

---

## Persona Response

Yeah, definitely. It's not occasional for me, it's more like a recurring practice at this point.

The way it works for me is: there are weeks where the news is manageable and I just absorb it through my feed without really making a choice. Then there are weeks where something bad and big is happening — a mass shooting, something political going wrong in a specific way, anything about housing or cost of living that feels like screaming into a void — and I notice myself consciously deciding to not look. I'll scroll past the news posts in my feed. I'll mute certain words. I'll avoid the YouTube news channels I usually find useful.

I'd say I go through this probably four or five times a month. There'll be a day or maybe two days where I'm deliberately not reading anything. It's not that I don't care. That's the thing I want to be clear about. Avoidance isn't the same as not caring. I care about Chicago housing. I care about what's happening politically. But there's a specific kind of despair that sets in when you've absorbed the same crisis information for the fourteenth time and nothing has changed. That despair isn't useful. So I cut it off.

The triggers are pretty specific: futility — when the story is big and I feel powerless; anxiety spikes — I know how my nervous system works now after a few years of paying attention to it; and a third thing which is harder to name but it's something like disgust at the coverage itself. When I feel like the news is performing outrage rather than informing me, I find it harder to engage even with the things I actually care about. The medium poisons the subject.

I've got notifications mostly off anyway. I deleted Twitter in 2020 and that was a big one. I still get some from BBC and Guardian but I tap through them selectively. I don't see this as a failure of civic engagement. I see it as managing a diet.
