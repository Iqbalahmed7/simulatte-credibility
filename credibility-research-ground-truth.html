Objective 01

Human Parity

Establishing how accurately Simulatte replicates the distribution of responses a real human sample would produce. This is the foundational credibility claim and the hardest to validate rigorously.

01

The core methodological challenge: You need real human responses to compare against, and they must be demographically matched to your simulation inputs. The three studies below represent increasing levels of rigor and credibility. Start with Study 1A (lowest cost, fastest), then build toward 1B and 1C.

Study 1A
Retrodictive Survey Replication
Start here Not started

Run published survey questions with known ground truth through Simulatte using a demographically matched synthetic population. Compare Simulatte's output distributions against the real published results.

Data sources
  • Pew Research Center public microdata (free)
  • YouGov / Gallup published datasets
  • American National Election Studies (ANES)
  • UK ONS social surveys
Primary metrics
  • Distribution Accuracy — % of responses correctly allocated per option (same metric as Artificial Societies)
  • Mean Absolute Error (MAE) per question
  • Human Benchmark ceiling — 91% (Stanford: 19% individual self-inconsistency)
Recommended scope
500–1,000 survey questions across diverse topics. Minimum 20–30 questions for a first proof-of-concept run.
Why it works
Ground truth is publicly available, replicable by third parties, and directly comparable to Artificial Societies' published methodology. Publishable on its own.
Estimated effort
Low — can be completed internally in weeks with no external partners required. Pew microdata is freely downloadable.
Key risk
Demographic matching quality — Simulatte's synthetic population must mirror the survey sample composition. Document this matching process carefully.
Study 1B
Prospective / Predictive Validation
Not started

The gold standard. Before real fieldwork is conducted, Simulatte predicts the outcome. Results are published only after actual survey data is collected and confirmed. Even 3–5 cases would be commercially compelling.

Approach options
  • Partner with a polling organisation — Simulatte predicts, they field, results confirmed
  • Use pre-registered election polling data (publicly verifiable outcomes)
  • Commission a small parallel real survey alongside a Simulatte simulation on the same topic
What makes this credible
Pre-registration at OSF (Open Science Framework) before results are known removes any accusation of cherry-picking. No other competitor has published prospective validation.
Estimated effort
Medium-high — requires an external partner or commissioned fieldwork. Timeline depends on partner availability.
Dependencies
Requires Study 1A to be complete first, to calibrate and validate the methodology before going prospective.

Differentiation opportunity: Artificial Societies has only published retrodictive results. A successful prospective study — especially one pre-registered — would immediately leapfrog their credibility claims.

Study 1C
Qualitative Resonance Testing
Not started

Run a real focus group on a topic. Separately, simulate the same group through Simulatte. Have human raters (blind) evaluate both transcripts. This tests qualitative depth — something no competitor currently measures.

Metrics
  • Thematic coverage overlap — do the same themes emerge?
  • Sentiment distribution match
  • Unexpected insight rate — does Simulatte surface non-obvious findings?
  • Blind rater "realness" score (human vs. simulated, can they tell?)
Why it matters
Competitors focus on survey accuracy. This stakes out a qualitative research use case that none of them are validating. Directly relevant to clients who use focus groups.
Estimated effort
Medium — requires recruiting and running a real focus group for comparison. Can be done with a client partnership to reduce cost.
Dependencies
None — can run in parallel with Study 1A. Does not require retrodictive accuracy methodology.

Study 2A
Direct Replication and Extension of Competitor Methodology
High priority Not started

Run the same distribution accuracy methodology as Artificial Societies on comparable survey data. Test Simulatte against raw GPT-4o, Claude, Gemini with simple synthetic persona prompts. Produce a direct, apples-to-apples table of results.

Test conditions
  • Simulatte (full platform)
  • GPT-4o — simple synthetic persona prompt
  • Claude Sonnet — simple synthetic persona prompt
  • Gemini 2.5 Pro — simple synthetic persona prompt
  • No-persona baseline (raw LLM, no persona framing)
Dataset
Use the same UC Berkeley / Pew-style survey data as Study 1A. Minimum 200 questions for statistical validity. Aim for 500+.
Output
A comparison table showing distribution accuracy and consistency score for each system. Publishable as a standalone white paper.
Dependencies
Overlaps significantly with Study 1A. Can be run concurrently — same dataset, add LLM conditions.
Study 2B
Persona Consistency Under Paraphrase
Not started

Ask the same question 50–100 different ways per persona across all test systems. Measure response consistency — the degree to which a persona holds stable views regardless of question framing. Artificial Societies claims 93%. Establish Simulatte's figure.

Primary metric
Response consistency: % of identical responses when the same semantic question is asked with different wording. Benchmark: 93% (Artificial Societies), upper bound ~81% (individual human ceiling).
Why this matters
Consistency is about reliability of individual personas, not just population accuracy. A high consistency score demonstrates that Simulatte personas have stable, coherent belief systems — not stochastic noise.
Study 2C
Stereotype and Hallucination Rate
Not started

Measure how often raw LLMs produce stereotyped or demographically flattened responses compared to Simulatte. Focus on niche or hard-to-reach demographic groups where LLM training data is thin and stereotyping risk is highest.

Test design
Ask LLMs and Simulatte to simulate niche groups (e.g., rural small-business owners aged 55–65, first-generation immigrant professionals). Compare variance in responses and alignment to known survey data for those groups.
Metric
Response variance within a group (higher is better — real groups have diversity), demographic accuracy vs. known group-level data, and a qualitative expert rating of stereotype presence.

Study 3A
Head-to-Head on Public Datasets
Not started

Run identical survey prompts through Simulatte and competitors using the same public dataset and methodology. Publish Simulatte's results independently. Frame against competitor claimed figures without requiring direct access to their systems.

Framing approach
Do not publish "Competitor X scored Y." Instead: "Using the same methodology as [citation], Simulatte achieves Z." Let readers draw their own comparisons. This is legally cleaner and more credible.
Ideal outcome
Simulatte's distribution accuracy exceeds 86% (Artificial Societies' published figure). If not, focus publication on other dimensions where Simulatte clearly leads.

Study 3C
Unique Use Case Validity — The Impossible Made Measurable
Not started

Document cases where traditional research failed and Simulatte delivered. Focus on hard-to-reach or commercially sensitive audiences: investors, senior policymakers, niche B2B segments. These cases do not require accuracy benchmarking — they demonstrate capability that competitors cannot claim.

Example use cases to document
  • Simulating institutional investor reactions to a comms narrative before publication
  • Testing policymaker responses to sensitive regulatory scenarios
  • Reaching niche professional segments that would be cost-prohibitive to survey at scale
  • Running research in markets where fieldwork is logistically or politically difficult
Publication format
Short client case studies (can be anonymised). 3–5 cases published together as a pattern of capability. Does not require quantitative accuracy claims.
