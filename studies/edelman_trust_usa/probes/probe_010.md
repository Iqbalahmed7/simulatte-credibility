## Transcript

**Stimulus delivered:** Your company has just announced a significant policy change that will affect how you work day-to-day. You haven't been consulted about it, and you have some concerns about how it will play out. A colleague asks you: "Do you trust that the company is making the right call here?" What's your honest answer?

**Marcus:**

My honest answer is: I trust that they have a reason, and I'm withholding judgment on whether the reason is a good one until I hear it.

Nine years here, and one thing I know is that decisions come from the top for reasons that don't always filter down immediately. Sometimes the reason turns out to be smart. Sometimes it turns out to be wrong. What I pay attention to is how they handle the follow-through — do they own it when something doesn't work, do they listen when people on the floor are telling them it's not landing right?

When I got passed over for the operations manager role in 2021, I was frustrated. I thought I was the right person for it. But the regional director called me personally — not HR, him — and he laid out a development path. Twelve months later I had the supervisor role and a nine-thousand-dollar raise. The process hurt. The follow-through was right. That's my template for how I assess what this company does.

So: do I trust them on this policy change? I trust their process more than I trust that this specific decision is perfect. I've watched enough policy changes roll out here to know that the ones that come without consultation sometimes get adjusted when the people implementing them say clearly what's not working. The question is whether there's a mechanism for that feedback to come back.

My concern right now is that they announced it without input from people like me — supervisors who are actually managing the thing they're changing. That's a gap. I've put in a request with my regional director to understand the reasoning. That's how I handle it.

I don't walk away from this organisation because a decision came from above without my input. I've seen what it looks like when a company isn't accountable — when they deny workers' comp, when they don't follow through on commitments. That's not this company. I measure them against a standard they've mostly met. Right now I'm waiting to see if they meet it again.
