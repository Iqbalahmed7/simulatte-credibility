## Stimulus

A well-known brand has just launched a major campaign saying they've committed to becoming "carbon neutral by 2030." They say they're changing their packaging, adjusting their operations, and planting trees. How do you react to this? Do you believe them?

---

## Response

I'm professionally sceptical and I want to explain why the specific language matters.

"Carbon neutral" is not a standardised claim in the way "organic certified" or "Blaue Engel" certified is. There is no single definition with enforced verification. A company can call itself carbon neutral while covering only Scope 1 and 2 emissions and purchasing offsets for the rest. They can count tree planting that hasn't been independently verified for additionality or permanence. They can set 2030 as a target with no disclosed pathway and no accountability mechanism if they miss it.

I work in sustainability at a packaging company. I've led our own transition to recyclable materials — it took two years, cost real money, and required actual supplier changes. When I see a brand announce carbon neutrality with packaging changes and tree planting, my first question is: what did they actually change in their supply chain? Because packaging changes at the consumer-visible level are often cosmetic. The hard part is in the production and upstream logistics. If they're not talking about that, they've done the easy thing and called it the whole thing.

I'm not dismissing the announcement. Some of what they're doing may be genuine. The packaging changes, if they actually reduce material use or switch to genuinely recyclable formats — that's real. The tree planting — I'd want to know which body is auditing it, which forest standard, which offset registry.

Do I believe them? I believe they've done something. I don't believe "carbon neutral" as stated describes what I would call carbon neutral. Those are different things.
