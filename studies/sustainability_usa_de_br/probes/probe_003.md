## Stimulus

How important is environmental sustainability to you personally? When you think about your day-to-day choices — what you buy, how you travel, what you eat — how much does environmental impact factor in?

---

## Response

It matters a great deal to me, and I think the honest answer requires me to separate what I believe from how I behave, because those two things aren't always the same.

At the level of conviction: very important. We live in Brazil. The environment here isn't an abstract international issue — it's the reason the rains come when they do, it's why São Paulo's water security has been in crisis, it's why some years the sky turns the colour it shouldn't. I grew up with this as background reality. The Amazon's health is connected to my health, to this city's weather, in ways that feel immediate and personal.

Day-to-day: it's mixed. I try to buy certified products when I find credible ones. I've been consistent with one cleaning brand that has third-party certification for about a year. I take public transit in the city — partly because driving in São Paulo would be insane, partly because it makes sense. I've reduced meat significantly.

Where I'm inconsistent: food delivery. I order Rappi or iFood maybe four times a week. The packaging waste is substantial. I've thought about this many times. I keep doing it. Convenience and the pattern of my work schedule beat the value I say I hold. That's the real answer to your question about daily choices.

I work in marketing, so I see sustainability communication from the inside. I know how "eco-friendly" language is written. When I buy something I'm looking for independent certification, not brand claims. Brand claims I basically discount by default — not completely, but I don't take them seriously without something external backing them.

So: very important in conviction, partially implemented in behaviour, with a specific named gap I haven't closed.

**2.** "Brand claims I basically discount by default" → Greenwashing scepticism is present but Fernanda frames it practically (she still bought certified products through discrimination), suggesting scepticism co-exists with purchase intent when certification is credible — consistent with F06 certification premium effect.

## Alternative Explanations

**Strongest alternative:** Fernanda's environmental concern narrative is shaped by her professional marketing role — her articulate scepticism about brand claims is as much a professional skill as a values-driven consumer disposition; an S05 persona in a non-marketing role might express similarly high concern with less analytical framing of the claim-construction mechanism.
