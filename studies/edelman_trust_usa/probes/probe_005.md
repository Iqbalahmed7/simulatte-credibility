## Transcript

**Stimulus delivered:** You're having a conversation with a neighbour about whether institutions in America can still be trusted to do the right thing. Your neighbour says: "I've just stopped paying attention — government, media, companies — they're all the same to me. None of them have my interests at heart." How do you respond? What's your own view?

**Margaret:**

I hear that. I really do. And I'm not going to tell you you're wrong, because in a lot of ways the frustration behind what you're saying is completely justified.

But — and I say this as someone who spent most of 2021 fighting a Medicare insurer at midnight — I can't fully agree. The frustration is right. The conclusion I think is a mistake, and here's why it matters to me personally.

When I spent those seven months dealing with my mother's insurance appeal, what I learned was that the system could be worked. Not easily, not fairly, not without privilege — I knew how to write a formal letter, I understood appeals processes, I had the time. That's real. But the mechanism existed. The appeal worked. If my mother didn't have me — and most people don't have a version of me — she would have lost that fight and paid twelve thousand dollars she didn't owe. That's a problem with access, not a problem with the existence of the mechanism itself.

What I resist is the move from "these institutions have failed many people" — which is true — to "they're all the same, none of it matters." Because I've watched that conclusion become its own kind of harm. When people disengage from government, the people who stay engaged fill the space. I've seen it in school board politics, I've seen it in local regulatory comment periods where one organised business group turns up and nobody else does. Cynicism isn't neutral. It leaves the field to people who aren't cynical.

I pay for journalism. I vote in primaries. I file Freedom of Information requests. I do those things not because I trust these institutions to perform well without pressure but because I think the pressure matters. There's a difference between trust as faith and trust as engagement. I have the second kind, which is more demanding and probably more honest.

So to your neighbour's question — I don't think they're all the same. I think business is more predictable than government right now, I think some media is doing excellent work and a lot of it isn't, and I think government is the most important one to stay engaged with even though it's the most frustrating. Giving up on it hands it to someone else.
