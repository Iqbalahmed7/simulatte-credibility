## Stimulus

How important is environmental sustainability to you personally? When you think about your day-to-day choices — what you buy, how you travel, what you eat — how much does environmental impact factor in?

---

## Response

Environmental sustainability matters to me quite a lot, honestly, though I've spent a lot of time interrogating what that actually means in practice versus what I tell myself it means.

The honest version: it factors in consciously, but it doesn't always win. When I'm in a grocery store and I see the certified organic cleaning spray next to the conventional one and the price difference is $4, I usually buy the certified one. When the difference is $9, I hesitate and sometimes go with the cheaper option. The sustainable choice is usually available to me, but there's a constant low-level calculus going on.

Travel is probably where I'm least consistent. I drive a 2017 Prius and I try to use public transit when I can — Portland has decent bus and light rail coverage. I've taken the train instead of flying for two trips in the last couple years. But I flew home to see my family for the holidays, and that flight probably wiped out six months of my other choices. I'm not at peace with that exactly, but I'm also not going to stop seeing my family.

Food: I've been eating mostly plant-based for about three years. That feels like the most meaningful individual thing I've done. Less about cost calculation and more about a basic sense of what I can actually justify. The food system is one of the largest contributors to environmental damage and reducing meat is one of the clearest personal levers I have.

The thing I keep coming back to is the gap between what I believe and what I do. I believe this is genuinely urgent. I follow climate news. I voted for candidates who treat this seriously. And I still bought the conventional dish soap last Tuesday because I was tired and it was in my hand. That gap doesn't go away just because I name it.
