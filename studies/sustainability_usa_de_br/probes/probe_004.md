## Stimulus

You're shopping for a household product — something you buy regularly, like cleaning supplies or food. You find two options on the shelf: one is your usual brand at the regular price, the other is labelled as sustainably produced and certified by an independent environmental organisation, but it costs 25% more. What do you do?

---

## Response

That scenario is basically my weekly grocery run, so I can be specific.

For cleaning products, I'm buying the certified one. Twenty-five percent more on a bottle of dish soap is a few dollars. If the certification is from an organisation I recognise — and I do have a mental list of which ones I take seriously — I'll pay it without much deliberation. I spend more than that on coffee I don't need.

For food, it depends. Produce — if we're talking a 25% premium on vegetables or basic staples, I'm doing a quick calculation: what's the category, what's the specific claim, and how tight is our budget that month. Since our daughter started daycare, the margin got tighter. For protein — meat, eggs — I've already moved mostly to a local butcher whose practices I know directly. That's more expensive across the board, so the question almost doesn't apply in that category.

The certification piece is genuinely important to me. I work in environmental compliance and I know how meaningless some environmental claims are at the company level. When there's independent third-party certification, it changes my calculus materially. I don't just want the company to say they're doing the right thing — I've read too many sustainability reports to take those at face value. The independent certification is the difference between a claim I can half-trust and one I can mostly-trust.

I'll also say: I'm more likely to buy the certified version if the label is familiar to me. If I see a certification logo I've never encountered, I'll look it up on my phone before I buy. I've done this several times.

What I don't do is buy the conventional version and feel fine about it. There's a low-grade discomfort that follows me if I skip the certified option when I could reasonably afford it. Not enough to stop me when budget genuinely prevents it, but it's there.
