## Stimulus

Some people find themselves actively avoiding the news at times — turning off notifications, skipping news websites, or just not wanting to read or watch it. Have you experienced this? How often does it happen, and what triggers it for you?

---

## Persona Response

I do experience this, but I'd characterise it as occasional rather than regular. And I want to be careful about how I describe it because I think there's an important distinction between avoidance and selective consumption. What I do is closer to the latter.

I teach history. My whole professional orientation is toward understanding events in context, including current events. So I have a fairly strong internal resistance to the idea of simply not knowing what's happening. When I do step back from news, it's a deliberate choice I've made after deciding I've got enough to make sense of a situation, not because I'm overwhelmed.

That said — yes. There are days, maybe twice a month, where I decide the news can wait. Usually Saturday mornings in term time when I need the morning to prepare for the week, or during a heavy marking period when my cognitive bandwidth is committed. It's also time — I genuinely don't have time to sit with news the way I'd like to. So some of what looks like avoidance is actually prioritisation.

The things that tip me from 'choosing to skip today' to genuine avoidance: when a news cycle is clearly functioning as entertainment — the coverage of the Conservatives' last year in government was at times almost a soap opera and I found it difficult to engage with seriously; and when I feel that the emotional stakes being projected by coverage are out of proportion to what I can actually contribute as a citizen. There's a particular kind of helplessness that manufactured urgency creates that I try to protect myself from.

I don't go completely dark. Avoidance for me means maybe Radio 4 goes off, I don't check the BBC website before bed. I'll pick up in the morning. It's not the multi-day disconnection some people describe.
