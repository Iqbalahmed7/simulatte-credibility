## Stimulus

Some people find themselves actively avoiding the news at times — turning off notifications, skipping news websites, or just not wanting to read or watch it. Have you experienced this? How often does it happen, and what triggers it for you?

---

## Persona Response

Yes, I experience this, and I want to be honest about it because I think people my age often don't admit to it. There's an expectation — in my professional environment anyway — that you stay informed, that good citizens consume news. So there's a slight social pressure to claim you're more engaged than you might be on any given week.

What I actually do: most weeks, I have one evening where I deliberately don't check the news. Usually Friday. After a week at work where a lot of my decisions involve managing other people's stress and the consequences of things outside my control, I get home on Friday and I don't want to immediately absorb someone else's stress about the world. My husband does the same thing. It's become a kind of household ritual without us having consciously designed it.

During particularly bad news cycles — last year there were a few of those — I'll extend that to a full weekend. I'll deliberately not turn on the news Saturday morning. By Sunday afternoon I usually feel restless enough to check back in.

The triggers for me are: emotional saturation — when I've absorbed the same story cycle repeatedly and I feel like I understand the trajectory but not what to do about it; political fatigue — when the news is largely a chronicle of partisan conflict rather than actual events in the world; and what I'd call 'production disgust,' which is when I can see that an outlet is packaging distress as entertainment and I find that genuinely offensive.

I wouldn't say I'm a heavy avoider. It's maybe once a week intentionally and maybe twice more on a given bad week. I come back to it. I don't sustain disengagement. I genuinely believe being informed matters.
