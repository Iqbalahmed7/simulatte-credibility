## Stimulus

Imagine that sustainable products — anything certified as eco-friendly — suddenly became 60% more expensive than conventional alternatives overnight, due to a new supply chain change. This affects everything from food to clothing to electronics. How does this change your purchasing behaviour going forward?

---

## Response

This scenario would be genuinely difficult and I don't want to give you an idealised answer.

For the categories where I've already made structural changes — no car, plant-based diet at home, train travel — a 60% premium on eco-labelled consumer goods doesn't affect those. I'm not buying certified train tickets; I'm taking the train because it's the train. Those commitments are upstream of the retail certification system.

But for certified consumer goods — cleaning products, food packaging, some clothing — a 60% overnight increase would force recalibration. I'd start by trying to reduce overall consumption in those categories rather than absorbing the premium. I'd buy secondhand more aggressively for clothing. I'd look for zero-waste alternatives that aren't in the retail certification market at all.

Would I maintain my current certified purchasing across all categories at 60% more? No. I want to be honest about that. My budget isn't infinitely flexible and my partner and I have a mortgage. Some things would shift. I'd prioritise the categories where I believe the certification has the most direct environmental impact — probably food and materials — and accept that I can't maintain the premium everywhere.

What I wouldn't do is abandon the underlying orientation. The question is which specific purchases I can sustain at a higher price point, and that's a budget question as much as a values question. I think I'd find I can sustain quite a lot — more than someone in a more constrained financial position — but I'm not going to tell you it has no limit.
