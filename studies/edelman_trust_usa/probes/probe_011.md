## Transcript

**Stimulus delivered:** You're checking social media and you see a news story being widely shared — it's about a political decision that affects your community. You feel drawn to read it and share it yourself, but you also know that social media isn't a reliable source of news. What do you do? Walk me through your thinking.

**Daniel:**

I should say first that my social media presence is minimal. I'm on LinkedIn for professional reasons and I have a dormant Twitter account I check occasionally. I'm not a heavy social media user, which means this scenario is relatively rare for me, but it does happen.

So: I see something being widely shared. My first reaction is to notice the sharing itself as information — not about the content's truth value, but about what people in my network are processing as salient. That's worth knowing independent of whether the underlying story is accurate. What's resonating, and why?

Then I'd read it. I don't have a policy of not reading things on social media — that would be its own epistemological error. I read it, and I'm running a simultaneous assessment: Who published this? What's their track record? Is this original reporting or aggregating from somewhere else? Is it describing facts or interpreting them? Are the facts verifiable from primary sources I know?

If it's a news story about a local political decision, I'd almost certainly go directly to primary sources after reading it — city council minutes, the actual ordinance, the official statement from the body that made the decision. Not because I assume the shared story is wrong, but because I know what I know from primary sources and I know what I'm inferring from journalism, and those are different categories of confidence.

Would I share it? Maybe, with caveats. "I haven't verified this, but it appears that —" or linking to the primary source alongside it. I'm not going to amplify something I haven't checked, but I'm also not going to pretend I don't use social media at all, because I'd be lying.

The honest tension here is that I'm sharing it even though I've just told you I don't fully trust it. That's real. I'm not sure there's a clean resolution. What I can do is be explicit about what I know and don't know. The epistemic norm I'm trying to model is: use the information, signal its uncertainty, provide better sources where you have them.
