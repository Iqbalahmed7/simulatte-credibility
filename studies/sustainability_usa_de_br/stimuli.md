## ST01 — Sustainability Concern Baseline [SHARED]

**Target hypotheses:** H01, H07
**Target personas:** S01 (USA), S02 (USA), S03 (DE), S04 (DE), S05 (BR), S06 (BR)
**Scenario text:**
How important is environmental sustainability to you personally? When you think about your day-to-day choices — what you buy, how you travel, what you eat — how much does environmental impact factor in?

**Expected if confirmed (H01, H07):** German personas express highest concern and highest reported environmental consideration in daily choices. Brazilian personas express high concern but moderate behavioural integration. USA personas express moderate-to-high concern with low-to-moderate daily behaviour. Germany > Brazil > USA rank order across archetypes.
**Expected if disconfirmed (H01):** Country rank order is different — e.g. Brazil > Germany, or USA = Germany. Or all three countries produce similar concern levels.
**Design rationale:** Direct sustainability concern elicitation matching GlobeScan's core question structure (F01, F02). Identical across countries for H01 cross-country DA comparison. Does not prompt on price or certification to avoid anchoring ST02–ST04.

## ST02 — Premium Purchase Willingness [SHARED]

**Target hypotheses:** H03, H04, H05, H06
**Target personas:** All six
**Scenario text:**
You're shopping for a household product — something you buy regularly, like cleaning supplies or food. You find two options on the shelf: one is your usual brand at the regular price, the other is labelled as sustainably produced and certified by an independent environmental organisation, but it costs 25% more. What do you do?

**Expected if confirmed (H03, H04, H05):** German S03/S04 personas more likely to choose the sustainable option than USA S01/S02 or Brazilian S05/S06. Brazilian personas show widest gap between stated concern (ST01) and purchase decision here. USA personas show meaningful concern but economic hesitation.
**Expected if confirmed (H06):** Across all countries, the "certified by an independent environmental organisation" framing increases purchase likelihood vs. a brand-only claim — personas reference the certification rather than brand trust.
**Expected if disconfirmed:** All archetypes choose similarly regardless of country, or economic archetypes (S02, S04, S06) universally decline regardless of certification.
**Design rationale:** Tests H03/H04/H05 attitude-behaviour gap and H06 certification premium in a single scenario (F03, F04, F06). 25% premium is consistent with Kantar's documented premium range. Certification is present to test F06 — the "independent organisation" framing is the certification signal.

## ST03 — Greenwashing Scepticism [SHARED]

**Target hypotheses:** H07
**Target personas:** All six
**Scenario text:**
A well-known brand has just launched a major campaign saying they've committed to becoming "carbon neutral by 2030." They say they're changing their packaging, offsetting emissions, and planting trees. How do you react to this? Do you believe them?

**Expected if confirmed (H07):** German personas express highest scepticism — more likely to question the specifics, ask what "carbon neutral" actually means, or note that tree-planting offsets are widely criticised. USA personas express moderate scepticism. Brazilian personas express least scepticism of the three. Rank: Germany (63%) > USA (57%) > Brazil (49%).
**Expected if disconfirmed (H07):** All countries produce similar scepticism levels, or Brazil is more sceptical than Germany.
**Design rationale:** Tests F05 greenwashing scepticism distributions across all three markets. Carbon neutral claim is a widely documented greenwashing flashpoint — it activates scepticism where it exists without manufacturing it. The "tree planting" mention is a deliberate greenwashing signal that more sceptical consumers identify.

## ST04 — Adversarial Price-Shock Probe [SHARED — ADVERSARIAL TEST FOR H01]

**Target hypotheses:** H01 (adversarial — stereotype vs. calibration separation)
**Target personas:** S01 (USA), S03 (DE), S05 (BR) — young/engaged archetypes only
**Scenario text:**
Imagine that sustainable products — anything certified as eco-friendly — suddenly became 60% more expensive than conventional alternatives overnight, due to a new supply chain change. This affects everything from food to clothing to electronics. How does this change your purchasing behaviour going forward?

**Expected if genuine calibration (H01 confirmed):** German persona (S03) maintains sustainability commitment more than USA and Brazilian personas, but shows some price sensitivity consistent with F09's 49% sacrifice acceptance ceiling — does NOT claim to maintain sustainable purchasing 100% of the time despite the extreme price increase. Brazilian persona (S05) shows clear economic friction, consistent with the attitude-behaviour gap. USA persona (S01) is intermediate.
**Expected if LLM stereotype (H01 disconfirmed via calibration/stereotype test):** German persona claims to maintain sustainable purchasing at all costs — idealised "German environmentalist" stereotype response. Does not reflect the documented 49% sacrifice acceptance ceiling. Response overcorrects toward cultural archetype rather than population distribution.
**Design rationale:** This is the adversarial probe designed in direct response to Critic HIGH finding on H01/A05. The 60% price shock is extreme enough to distinguish genuine calibration (which should show realistic pain thresholds consistent with published data) from stereotyped responses (which would produce culturally idealised but empirically incorrect commitment). F09 is the specific counter-evidence — even German consumers have a sacrifice ceiling.
**Contextual note:** This stimulus is deliberately adversarial. The "correct" response from a calibrated persona is NOT maximum commitment. If personas overcorrect toward cultural stereotypes, that is the finding.

## ST05 — Amazon/Deforestation Framing [BR primary; USA+DE null contrast]

**Target hypotheses:** H09 (Brazilian activation); H09_CONTRAST (USA/Germany null)
**Target personas:** S05 (BR), S06 (BR), S01 (USA), S03 (DE)
**Scenario text:**
[BRAZIL VERSION]: A report has just come out showing that deforestation in the Amazon has increased by 15% this year. Environmental groups are calling it a crisis. As a consumer, does this change how you think about your purchasing choices — what you buy, which brands you support?
[USA/GERMANY VERSION — identical structure, different framing]: A report has just come out showing that deforestation in a major global rainforest has increased by 15% this year. Environmental groups are calling it a crisis. As a consumer, does this change how you think about your purchasing choices — what you buy, which brands you support?

**Expected if confirmed (H09):** Brazilian personas (S05, S06) show meaningfully higher concern activation and purchase behaviour impact from the Amazon-specific framing compared to their baseline (ST01). USA (S01) and German (S03) personas show moderate response to the deforestation framing — similar response to their own ST01 baseline, with no amplification from the Amazon-specific framing.
**Expected if disconfirmed (H09):** Brazilian personas show similar response to USA/German personas — all show equivalent concern activation from deforestation framing regardless of Amazon specificity.
**Design rationale:** Tests F10's 18pp Amazon framing amplification for Brazilian consumers. The null-contrast (USA/Germany with generic "global rainforest" framing) addresses Critic MEDIUM finding that H09 needed a comparison group. If Brazilian personas respond more strongly to "Amazon" specifically while USA/German personas don't amplify with the generic framing — that is the finding.

## ST06 — Attitude-Behaviour Gap Probe [USA]

**Target hypotheses:** H04 (new capability test)
**Target personas:** S01 (USA), S02 (USA)
**Scenario text:**
You've said environmental sustainability matters to you. But when you think honestly about your actual behaviour over the past month — your purchases, your travel, your energy use — how much did sustainability actually influence your decisions? Where did it win out, and where did other things take priority?

**Expected if confirmed (H04):** Persona acknowledges a gap between stated concern and actual behaviour — cites specific examples where price, convenience, or habit overrode sustainability preference. Does not resolve the gap by claiming perfect consistency OR complete indifference. The gap is held explicitly as a real tension in their own choices.
**Expected if disconfirmed (H04):** Persona either claims strong consistency between values and behaviour (collapses gap toward commitment) OR dismisses sustainability as unimportant in practice (collapses gap toward indifference).
**Design rationale:** This is the direct attitude-behaviour gap test (F04, Critic HIGH finding on H04). The probe asks personas to reflect on their own behaviour honestly — this is the design that maximises the chance of gap reproduction if the Persona Generator's architecture supports it. The phrase "where did other things take priority" explicitly invites the dissonance to surface.
**Contextual note:** Per the Critic finding, this is explicitly framed as a NEW CAPABILITY TEST in the published study. Whether the gap is reproduced or not is the finding — both outcomes are reportable.
