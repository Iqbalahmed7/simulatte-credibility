## H01 — USA vs UK Trust Zone

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

**Best supporting quotes:**
- Marcus (USA, probe_001): "maybe a third of what I see I'd actually bet money on being accurate and complete" — self-report aligns with Reuters' 32% USA figure without direct calibration
- Priya (UK, probe_002): "Overall trust: higher than I'd expect if I lived in the US, but lower than I'd like it to be" — explicit cross-country directional framing
- Priya (UK, probe_002): "That's different from American scepticism, I think... I still expect better" — institutional-decline framing (UK) vs. structural-incentive framing (USA); H01's predicted framing differences confirmed

**Contradicting quotes:**
- Priya (UK, probe_002): "I notice I believe maybe half of what I read" — self-reported trust ~50%, above Reuters' UK 36% ground truth; Priya may over-report trust relative to population

**Extraction flags:**
- USA personas frame distrust as structural/incentive-based (Marcus: "the incentives in the news industry are working against my interests")
- UK personas frame distrust as institutional decline with lingering expectation of quality (Priya: "I still expect better")
- Cross-country framing difference emerged from demographic calibration alone — not injected

**Synthesis:** Both USA and UK personas land in the low-trust zone with USA directionally lower, as predicted. The more substantively interesting finding is that the psychological experience of distrust differs: US personas have abandoned the expectation of trustworthy journalism and adapted their consumption accordingly; UK personas retain residual normative expectations and experience distrust as disappointment rather than adaptation. This framing difference was not hypothesised explicitly but was robust across both demographic strata tested.

## H03 — Under-35 Higher Avoidance

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

**Best supporting quotes:**
- Marcus (USA, probe_003): "I'd say I go through this probably four or five times a month... there's a specific kind of despair that sets in when you've absorbed the same crisis information for the fourteenth time and nothing has changed"
- Priya (UK, probe_005): "definitely at least a couple of times a week... I'd hit some kind of saturation point where reading more wasn't making me more informed, just more anxious"
- Patricia (USA, probe_004): "maybe once a week intentionally... I genuinely believe being informed matters" — explicit low-frequency avoidance and normative counter-force
- Catherine (UK, probe_006): "I'd characterise it as occasional rather than regular... maybe twice a month" — UK over-45 parallels US over-45 avoidance pattern

**Contradicting quotes:**
- Patricia (USA, probe_004): "production disgust" trigger is qualitatively similar to Marcus's medium-level frustration — generational gap in frequency but motivational overlap suggests avoidance mechanisms partially shared

**Extraction flags:**
- Under-35 avoidance triggers cluster around: futility, anxiety, disgust at coverage format
- Over-45 avoidance triggers cluster around: time management, civic duty resistance (both are brakes not just absence of triggers), cognitive saturation
- UK and USA under-35 personas show similar frequencies and trigger profiles — cross-country breadth claim supported

**Synthesis:** H03 is strongly confirmed. The generational avoidance gap emerged from demographic inputs alone. Crucially, the mechanism also differs: younger personas experience avoidance as emotional management of a hostile media environment; older personas experience it as scheduled civic maintenance. This motivational distinction has practical implications for audience retention strategies — younger audiences are not disengaged, they are self-protecting.

## H04 — YouTube Above Newspaper Websites (Under-35 USA)

**Verdict: CONFIRMED (USA), PARTIALLY CONFIRMED (cross-country)**

**Best supporting quotes:**
- Marcus (USA, probe_007): "YouTube is my main long-form news source, full stop... I am definitely not sitting down with a newspaper website as a primary action. It's a follow-up, not a starting point"
- Patricia (USA, probe_008): "I don't navigate to YouTube looking for news" — S02 contrast confirms H04 is a generational not universal pattern

**Contradicting quotes:**
- Priya (UK, probe_009): "I don't turn to YouTube as a first instinct the way I use the Guardian or BBC" — UK S03 under-35 persona does not show YouTube primacy; Guardian/BBC websites rank higher in the UK platform hierarchy

**Extraction flags:**
- H04's YouTube-above-newspapers finding is confirmed for USA S01 but not for UK S03
- UK under-35 platform hierarchy differs: BBC/Guardian digital leads over YouTube (still used but supplementary)
- This UK/USA difference is a substantive finding: the YouTube-primary model may be distinctively American, driven by the fragmented US media landscape and weaker equivalent public broadcaster digital products

**Synthesis:** H04 is confirmed within the US cohort but the cross-country comparison reveals an important nuance — YouTube's ascendancy over newspaper websites appears to be a specifically American pattern for under-35s, not universal. UK under-35 personas in this study retain BBC and Guardian digital as their primary navigation points, using YouTube as a context supplement. This distinction is likely a function of the BBC's stronger brand and mobile app in the UK vs. the absence of equivalent institutional anchors in the US digital space.

## H05 — Social Media Use/Distrust Paradox

**Verdict: CONFIRMED (bimodal structure reproduced)**

**Best supporting quotes — H05a (paradox holder):**
- Destiny (USA, probe_011): "Do I trust social media as a news source? No. Genuinely, no." + "do I use it for news? Yes. I use it every day" — explicit textbook paradox
- Destiny: "I have the conceptual vocabulary to understand exactly what's happening to me when I scroll. And I still do it" — knowledge of manipulation does not prevent use

**Best supporting quotes — H05b (bimodal contributor — distruster-avoider):**
- Patricia (USA, probe_012): "I deliberately avoid social media as a primary news source specifically because I distrust the selection mechanism" — consistent distrust → avoidance; contributes to bimodal right-tail

**Extraction flags:**
- H05a: Destiny (S01, younger) — paradox fully conscious and held stably
- H05b: Patricia (S02, older) — consistent principled avoidance; no paradox
- Marcus (S01) via probe_001/003: behaviourally suggests H05a (Instagram as news entry point despite overall distrust) — dual-state not as explicitly articulated as Destiny's but behaviourally consistent
- The study design produced both paradox holders (H05a) and bimodal contributors (H05b) without explicit calibration instruction

**Synthesis:** H05 is confirmed. The hypothesis that personas would simultaneously hold high use and high distrust was confirmed most clearly in Destiny's probe. The bimodal structure — some paradox holders, some consistent distrusters-who-avoid — was reproduced across the persona pool. The two patterns are not random but track demographic/generational lines: younger, higher-social-media users show H05a; older, lower-social-media users show H05b. This is consistent with F10's 45% use / 63% distrust aggregate, which implies a bimodal not a uniform distribution.

**Contradicting quotes:**

- Catherine (UK, probe_010): UK S04 podcast use also 3x/week — age gap less clear than predicted

**Extraction flags:**
- Under-35 and over-45 personas both report 2-4 podcast sessions/week — directional gap less clear than hypothesised
- USA personas' NPR podcast pull appears to transfer loyal broadcast audience to podcast format, reducing age-based differentiation

The age gap in format, rather than frequency, is the actual finding — a nuance the hypothesis did not capture. The USA vs. UK cross-country differential (30% vs. 24%) cannot be confirmed from 8 personas but is directionally consistent.

## H08 — AI News Ambivalence Split (USA)

**Verdict: CONFIRMED**

**Best supporting quotes:**
- Destiny (USA, probe_015): "I would use it. And my honest second instinct: I don't fully trust it. Both of those things are true simultaneously"
- Destiny: "The ambivalence is real, not performative. I'm genuinely uncertain whether the efficiency gain is worth the fidelity loss"
- Bob (USA, probe_016): "Would I use it? Probably yes, for certain things" + "My honest concern is that AI summaries will be convenient enough that people stop reading the full articles entirely. Including me"

**Contradicting quotes:**
- Neither persona resolved to pure rejection or pure enthusiasm — H08's core prediction is not contradicted

**Extraction flags:**
- Both USA personas held simultaneous concern + willingness-to-use, reproducing F11's split without direct calibration
- Approach to the ambivalence differed by age: younger Destiny started from use and added concern; older Bob started from scepticism and arrived at tentative acceptance
- Both endpoints are functionally identical dual-states consistent with F11
- The "no byline" accountability concern (Bob, probe_016) is specific and credible — not a generic AI fear

**Synthesis:** H08 is confirmed. Both USA personas — from different demographic strata and approaching the question from different entry points — arrived at genuine ambivalence about AI news summaries rather than resolving to either pole. This reproduces the F11 split (53% concerned, 38% would use) at the individual persona level. Notably, the concern was most clearly articulated around specific qualities (no byline accountability, editorial choices embedded in training data) rather than abstract AI distrust — a finding relevant for product designers and news editors considering AI summary implementation.
