## Stimulus

You're shopping for a household product — something you buy regularly, like cleaning supplies or food. You find two options on the shelf: one is your usual brand at the regular price, the other is labelled as sustainably produced and certified by an independent environmental organisation, but it costs 25% more. What do you do?

---

## Response

I take the regular one. That's the simple answer.

I don't have room in my budget for a 25% premium. When I go to the Carrefour or the Extra in Osasco, I know what things cost and I know what I can spend that week. My dish soap runs out every month. I buy the same one I've bought for years. If the eco one is 25% more, that's something like two or three reais more per bottle. That's two bus fares for me or a packet of rice. I'm not making a trade-off between a soap brand and rice to feel better about the environment.

I want to be honest: I'm not against this kind of certification. If someone told me it was real, that it actually means the product is better, I'd believe them. I think the environment matters. I grew up near a river and I know what happens when water gets polluted. But believing in something and being able to act on it financially are very different things.

The people who buy the expensive eco products in this country — I clean their houses. I see what they buy. They earn twenty, thirty thousand reais a month. It's not comparable.

If the price were the same, I'd choose the certified one. If it were a few centavos more, I'd think about it. At 25% more, no.

## Contradictions Found

**1.** "If the price were the same, I'd choose the certified one" → Claudia demonstrates that her non-purchase is income-driven, not values-indifferent — consistent with H03's hypothesis that Brazil's gap is income-constrained; this also partially challenges the interpretation that low purchase rates reflect low concern.

**2.** "The people who buy the expensive eco products in this country — I clean their houses" → Claudia's direct class observation frames the sustainability consumer market as income-stratified; this is a structural comment that reveals she sees herself as excluded from the premium-product conversation by design, not by preference.

## Alternative Explanations

**Strongest alternative:** Claudia's explicit statement "If the price were the same, I'd choose the certified one" may overstate her latent certification preference — in practice, habit, brand familiarity, and availability may drive purchase decisions beyond price, meaning even zero-premium certification might not increase her uptake.
